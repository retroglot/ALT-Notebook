# Mozart and Metadata: Classical Music in the Streaming Age

> RYAN BLAKELEY

In August 2021 the multi-trillion-dollar tech company Apple acquired the classical music streaming service Primephonic and announced plans to launch its own dedicated classical service.1 Apple Music Classical officially launched a year and a half later in March 2023 as a stand-alone app promising to "deliver the listening experience classical music lovers deserve."2 The service, available to existing Apple Music subscribers at no extra cost, advertised itself as having the biggest classical catalog, the highest audio quality, an improved search engine and user interface, expert recommendations, robust editorial content, and more.

Apple Music Classical is part of a growing market of specialized classical music services, which include Idagio, Presto Music, Deutsche Grammophon's Stage+, Tempso, and Vialma. These services aim to improve the classical music streaming experience and typically focus exclusively on classical repertoire, as opposed to the "full catalog" on mainstream platforms. On most streaming services—as on some radio stations—"classical music" is treated as a marketing category that broadly encompasses Western art music and related styles, such as orchestral film scores and video game soundtracks. For clarity and consistency, I similarly use the term "classical music" in this article to refer to the wide range of aesthetic practices, historical eras, and cultural values associated with Western art music, from early repertoires to contemporary instrumental and operatic works.

The large number of specialized classical music streaming services reflects the genre's unique position in today's pop-oriented streaming market. While streaming platforms like Spotify have been the largest source of record industry revenue worldwide since the mid-2010s, classical music has not experienced the same growth. In part, this is because mainstream services are not built with Western art music in mind: the metadata frameworks for their search and recommendation engines are ineffective for classical repertoires, they facilitate passive listening experiences that are seemingly at odds with this music's historical emphasis on active listening, and they lack guidance for new listeners who may be overwhelmed by the genre.

While classical music streaming services like Idagio and Presto Music are an attractive alternative to mainstream platforms, such genre-specific services are precarious. Not only are they often predicated on acquiring venture capital—which requires proving market value and growth potential, despite a limited potential userbase—but they are also competing against massive tech companies like Apple, Amazon, Google, Spotify, and Tencent that already have the streaming market cornered. For this reason, many previous attempts at classical music streaming services have either shut down (e.g., Composed, DG Discovery, and Grammofy) or have been acquired (e.g., Primephonic). Apple Music Classical, as an extension of Apple Music, occupies a less tenuous position, but reflects the increasing concentration of power over music streaming in just a handful of companies.

The interrelationship of classical music, new media, and industry has long been an important site of musicological inquiry. From the printing press to the phonograph, new technologies have influenced Western art music, including the way it is composed, performed, circulated, listened to, and commodified. Certain technological developments in the twentieth century, such as the increased audio fidelity and storage capacity of the LP and CD, were designed explicitly to accommodate expansive classical works like symphonies. But music's move online represented a shift in priorities, in that it was not motivated by classical music or its listeners. As musicologist Arved Ashby claims, "art music has become just as technology-dependent as pop, but has long since ceased to dictate format or market decisions." While scholars have extensively researched how streaming services such as Spotify and Apple Music are reshaping the music industry and affecting the way people find, access, and listen to music, the effects of streaming platforms on classical music have not received the same attention. Streaming affects many genres in distinct ways, but classical music's long-standing associations with "high art" and its aesthetic and cultural distinction from popular music mean that it has been especially impacted by the pop-driven streaming paradigm.

In this article, I investigate how streaming is shaping the business and culture of classical music. To do so, I analyze and compare streaming services' programming, interfaces, features, and marketing materials, supplementing my findings with insights drawn from interviews with the CEOs of several classical music streaming services conducted between 2021 and 2023.<sup>9</sup> I begin by examining how metadata affects the ways in which users access and experience classical music on streaming services. I demonstrate that mainstream platforms' search and recommendation engines are often inadequate for classical music, prompting niche classical services to build their own proprietary metadata databases. This leads to a discussion of the way features like mood-based playlists and algorithmic recommendations—typically associated with popular music on mainstream platforms—both shape and reflect classical music listening practices and ideologies. While most streaming services engender passive listening experiences that do not align with classical music's association with active listening, niche services attempt to accommodate more diverse audiences through both active and passive listening features. Next, I address how streaming's royalty system disadvantages classical musicians and labels, leading classical services to devise alternative royalty models and revenue streams. The article closes with a coda on platforms and power that considers the control that tech companies and major record labels hold over classical music's circulation and consumption.

# Mired in Metadata

Imagine that you are new to classical music. You have heard that Beethoven's Ninth Symphony is an important work, so you open Spotify's web client and type "beethoven symphony 9" into the search bar. Many of the challenges classical music faces on streaming services can be distilled into a single word: metadata. Often defined as "data about data," metadata plays a critical role in organizing and contextualizing data on digital platforms.[12] On music streaming services, metadata includes basic context such as the artist, album, track title, release date, and genre, but also more complicated details such as musical characteristics, usage statistics, and how royalties should be split among rightsholders. Streaming services typically store metadata in centralized databases, which are compiled from data supplied by labels, distributors, and metadata providers.[13] Though often out of sight for users, metadata drives many of the features that characterize digital platforms and the music streaming era: search engines capable of navigating massive catalogs, personalized algorithmic recommendations that predict taste, and royalty payments based on how often tracks are streamed.

Metadata is crucial for all genres on streaming services, but classical music faces particular challenges. $^{14}$ Major platforms like Spotify and Apple Music are designed to appeal to the widest audience possible, with the result that their metadata frameworks are oriented around parameters most useful for categorizing popular music: artist, album, and song. While such an approach is effective for most popular music, classical music generally does not adhere to this model. Instead, it demands detailed distinctions in terms of works (generic names, programmatic titles, and nicknames), movement labels, opus numbers, composers, conductors, orchestras, soloists, eras, and so forth. This means that metadata is forced into a limited set of ill-fitting parameters, often resulting in missing or overlapping information. For example, a single work may be associated with several "artists" (composer, orchestra, soloist, etc.) whose roles are not distinguished from one another, or may be represented by the most famous name only. Furthermore, in classical music the ontological distinction between a "work" and a "recording" is much greater than in most popular music, and streaming services are not equipped to sort the hundreds of interpretations that canonical works receive.[15] For these reasons, most streaming services are notoriously ineffective for classical music when it comes to their user interfaces, search engines, and recommendations—as illustrated by the Spotify search described above.[16] This issue is not new: classical music's metadata problem has been documented in journalistic publications like the New York Times, the New Yorker, and NPR since the mid-2010s.[17]

To fully understand classical music's metadata problem in the streaming age, however, it is necessary to trace its evolution from the CD era. Jeremy Wade Morris investigates how music metadata technologies and databases in the 1990s—such as the open-source Compact Disc Database (CDDB) for CDs and the ID3 metadata container for MP3s—were largely developed by hobbyists with data crowdsourced from users.[18] During this period, metadata primarily provided text-based information about music, helping listeners catalog and navigate their digital music libraries in media players like Apple's iTunes.

Morris argues that the musical tastes of those initially responsible for providing music metadata—typically young, white, tech-savvy men—resulted in systems that inherently privileged genres of music such as rock, pop, electronica, and hip-hop over classical music, jazz, and non-Western musics. Consequently, metadata bases and media players like iTunes were not programmed to accommodate classical music's complex metadata requirements.[19] In Morris's words, "while there are various plausible explanations (e.g., demographics, fidelity), classical music faces fundamental informational challenges that do not apply to other genres. Metadata, despite its flexibility and expandability, was not designed with classical music in mind."[20] He expands on the problem elsewhere, noting that "the issues metadata pose for jazz and classical music highlight the problems that arise when databases and tags built for certain resources start accommodating objects that require different sorting strategies. They remind us that ID3 tags and the CDDB are built on culturally inflected categories and attributes that are tied to the social setting in which those technologies evolved."[21] This is important because metadata actively shapes how users engage with music: "by filling in music's missing information, metadata describe and prescribe music collections on computers. They are keystone technologies that hold various other technologies in place and help them interact. In short, metadata mediate listeners' experiences with music."[22]

Morris primarily focuses on an era in which CDs and digital downloads were dominant, but metadata has grown even more important in the data-driven streaming paradigm. Instead of organizing a listener's personal music library, metadata on streaming services plays a key role in personalizing recommendations and searching music within a database of over one hundred million tracks.[23] Because metadata mediates listening experiences, classical music's metadata problem is more than just an inconvenience: committed users may find what they are looking for despite the hindrances, but others may find the experience too frustrating and either give up or seek alternatives. For those new to classical music, an already intimidating genre may feel impossible to approach. If users are dissatisfied with their experience, they may be dissuaded from listening to such music on streaming platforms, which might then reduce the genre's visibility, listenership, and revenue.

To address metadata problems, many niche classical streaming services built proprietary metadata bases based on classical music's informational requirements. Lukas Krohn-Grimberghe, former CEO and founder of the now defunct service Grammofy, tells me that the company's metadata "was the core of our product, really; having a database and metadata structure not based on pop music but based on all music.... We said we cannot build an intuitive, good user interface unless we have really good metadata."[24] Pierre Pariente, CEO and co-founder of the classical service Tempso, similarly says that "this database stuff was at every moment in our exploration the guiding principle. We're not going to compromise on that because we're literally building something for classical music. So we're not going to take shortcuts or ignore edge cases because that would entirely remove the purpose of what we're doing. That took a long time and it was difficult."[25]

Part of the challenge of building a new metadata framework for classical music is that the information supplied to streaming services by record labels, distributors, and metadata providers is not formatted for classical music and is frequently incomplete, inconsistent, or inaccurate. Building a classical music metadata base for streaming is a laborious and time-consuming process that requires manually inputting information. Till Janczukowicz, founder and CEO of Idagio and a former classical artist manager, claims that "for more than two years we had more than forty additional people who weren't doing anything else than inserting label repertoire by hand. We did build ingestion pipelines and technology to facilitate and shortcut, but it was human work."[26] Primephonic's former CEO Thomas Steffens (now Business Development at Apple Music) similarly informs me that it took the company several years to develop its metadata base.[27] While such statements are a strategic way for CEOs to demonstrate the labor required to build their services—and thus emphasize their worth—they are also a reminder that streaming services, like most digital platforms, derive much of their value from data.[28]

These genre-specific metadatabases result in more intuitive user interfaces for browsing, searching, and discovering classical music. Typing "beethoven symphony 9" in Idagio's web client or mobile app returns not only an artist page for Beethoven but also a separate page for the "work" that allows users to browse hundreds of recordings of the symphony via featured conductors, ensembles, soloists, popularity, release date, and so on, as seen in figure 3.[29] Furthermore, Idagio's interface is designed to accommodate classical music searches based on metadata distinctions that are not available on mainstream services like Spotify; the service's browse tab allows users to sort music by composers, performers, periods, genres, and instruments. Other classical services, like Apple Music Classical and Presto Music, likewise offer navigational tools more tailored to classical music.

It is telling that none of the major streaming services have implemented a better metadata framework for classical music, with the exception of Apple Music Classical (which inherited its metadata from Primephonic). It may be that classical music is too different to exist comfortably alongside other types of music in an all-purpose streaming service, but the fact that most mainstream services have not attempted to solve the genre's metadata problem also suggests that classical music is perceived as a niche market that is not economically worth the effort of fixing. (According to a 2024 report from the music data company Luminate, classical music accounts for less than 1 percent of music consumption in the US.)<sup>30</sup> While a focus on classical music may strategically align with Apple's brand image of quality and prestige, offering one explanation for its investment in the genre, for other streaming services it seems that the classical userbase is not large enough to warrant special attention.<sup>31</sup> Both Grammofy's Krohn-Grimberghe and Tempso's Pariente told me that they had approached Spotify with an offer to collaborate on a better classical music experience, but were told that they would have to demonstrate that there was a substantial enough userbase before the company would consider it.<sup>32</sup>

Classical music's metadata problem reveals that ideas about music and its usage are built into streaming services at a technical level, from their back-end infrastructures to their front-end interfaces. Western art music and other genres that do not fit neatly into the popular music framework—including jazz, experimental rock, and various traditional musics—are often marginalized and adversely affected on mainstream platforms, potentially reducing their visibility and viability. Even if major platforms such as Spotify, Amazon Music, and YouTube Music were to suddenly improve their classical music metadata, that would not change the fact that their programming and business models (developed largely to support and market popular music) would continue to have a direct impact on the way classical music is discovered and listened to.

# From Concert Hall to Playlist

Like all media, digital platforms' affordances influence engagement. If metadata frameworks represent beliefs about how music should be classified and contextualized, then the features and interfaces of streaming services reflect attitudes about listening.[33] Robert Prey compares the effects of streaming services to those of concert halls, suggesting that "just as nineteenth-century 'classical' music cannot be understood apart from the concert halls that mediated both music and ideas about attentive listening, contemporary music, the business of listening, and the meanings attributed to both will be intimately tied to platforms."34

Concert halls and streaming services represent vastly different listening ideals. Prey argues that "if a nineteenth-century concert hall can be understood as the materialization of ideals about attentive listening, then streaming platforms can be said to build 'inattentive listening' into their service."35 Indeed, many scholars and journalists discuss the way streaming services engender "passive" or "lean back" listening experiences via mood- and activity-based playlists, algorithmic recommendations, and autplay features.36 While these features have proven immensely successful for a userbase that primarily consumes popular music, they seemingly clash with the attentive listening practices historically associated with classical music.

Attentive listening is not genre-specific, but it does have particularly strong ties to classical music culture. In Europe during the early to mid-nineteenth century, the rise of art religion and the privileging of structural listening established a set of "concert hall ideals" according to which silent audiences were meant to listen to performances with rapt attention.[37] Darryl Cressman argues that such attentive listening has been integral to shaping the "high art" status of European concert music, claiming that it reinforces notions of "aesthetic autonomy, the cult of the genius composer, the ritual of the public concert, [and] the superiority of instrumental music." As he puts it, "all of these dimensions of [classical] musical culture are legitimated by attentive listening."[38] Attentive listening is not inherently better than passive listening—after all, listening ideologies are social constructs shaped by factors such as class, gender, and race[39]—but it has played an important role in defining the traditions and values associated with classical music.

Classical music's ideology of attentive listening developed prior to recording technology, but endured as music was brought from the concert hall into the living room.[40] In the early days of recording, classical music record producers and engineers sought to replicate the experience of listening to music live, assuming focused attention from listeners even in domestic spaces.[41] In his material history of classical music recording, Colin Symes quotes the famous EMI classical music producer Walter Legge, who "declared that all recordings of classical music should sound in the home as if they were being heard 'in the best seat in an acoustically perfect hall.'" Symes reflects that "part of the classical recording industry's failure to depart from the proscenium arch of the concert hall must be attributed to the fact that it is performers and composers who wield most of the power in the classical music community, and who determined, early on, that the phonograph should defer to their musical values—not the other way around."[42] While there are now many different approaches to recording and producing classical music, the ideology of the concert hall remains common, particularly for canonical works.[43]

This is not to say that audiences always listened to concert music attentively during the nineteenth century and have done so since, or that attention has been conceived as a static modality.[44] In concert settings, listening behavior could vary dramatically according to genre, seating, and other factors. For instance, concertgoers might listen to instrumental music differently from the way they listened to opera, just as those sitting in an opera box might listen differently from those in the orchestra stalls. During the twentieth century, when classical recordings began to be used as background music (for example, on film soundtracks, "mood music" LPs, "relaxing classical music" CDs, car radios, and in shopping malls), passive audition was often implied.[45] But although a variety of listening practices can be associated with the European concert repertoire, ideologies of active listening—even when subverted—have nevertheless persisted in defining its high art status.

If attentive audition legitimizes much of classical music culture, then what happens in a digital age that promotes passive and fragmented listening experiences? Discussing the rise of the iPod and MP3s in the early 2000s, Ashby considers how playlists, the shuffle feature, and the increased accessibility of recordings encourage the dissolution of the work concept and a destabilizing of the canon.[46] He claims that "if limitations of access help define or at least perpetuate a canon, proliferation of authors and texts begets a fragmented market," going on to ponder, "Could absolute music have ever become such a far-reaching cultural construct if nineteenth-century music-lovers had had access to a near-infinite number of disposable $99\text{€}$ music downloads and the almost immediate turnover of taste and aesthetic that they encourage?"[47]

Ashby's comments predate the streaming paradigm by a few years, but on-demand streaming platforms have only intensified the questions he raises. Although there is nothing to prevent users from listening intently to a classical recording on a platform like Spotify—barring the aforementioned metadata problems—streaming services generally privilege passive listening in order to maintain continuous engagement (and thereby generate advertising revenue and user data).48 In particular, Spotify's search engine and recommendations promote human-curated and algorithmically generated playlists, many of which are based on particular moods, activities, or "vibes."49 These playlists are designed to be listened to in the background to elicit affective responses, an example of what Anahid Kassabian calls "ubiquitous listening."50 Spotify's genre page for classical music prominently foregrounds dozens of such playlists, including "Calming Classical," "Classical Cooking," "Feel Good Classical," "Soundtracks for Studying," and "Dark Academia Classical." Other playlists allow users to discover "Classical Essentials," "Classical New Releases," and the output of individual composers (e.g., "This is Mozart").

In classical music playlists, different styles, movements, composers, performers, ensemble types, regions, and historical eras are jumbled together under an overarching theme. To some degree, these fragmented playlists mark a return to nineteenth-century mixed programming, in which individual movements, songs, and operatic extracts might be intermingled. But they also undermine historical musical values and practices by decontextualizing composers and compositions, subverting concepts of the musical work, artistic autonomy, and the cult of the composer. There are also aesthetic implications: because playlists are often designed to be played in the background, instrumental repertoire with a limited dynamic range—especially certain styles of solo piano music, such as that of Ludovico Einaudi—tends to be programmed more frequently on mood and activity playlists than classical vocal music or pieces with greater expressive contrasts.

Classical playlists are also in some ways extensions of programming trends formed via twentieth-century media. Perhaps the closest parallel is classical music radio—whether terrestrial, satellite, or internet—where recordings are continuously transmitted across one or more channels. (It is no coincidence that many streaming services use the word "radio" or "station" to describe their endless algorithmic playlists, such as

Pandora Radio, Spotify Radio, or Apple Music's Discovery Station.) While programming can vary significantly between classical radio stations, those aiming for mass appeal—such as Classic FM, KUSC, and ABC Classical—are often designed for background consumption, and thus regularly feature individual movements or excerpts from larger works. They also adopt a broad approach to "classical music" that spans eras, genres, and styles (including orchestral film and video game scores).[51] Unlike on-demand streaming services, however, radio stations are typically broadcast in real time and are non-interactive (meaning that users cannot manually select, sequence, and skip tracks). Additionally, classical music radio typically features human hosts and places a greater emphasis on curated programming than on personalized algorithmic recommendations, avoiding some of the more fragmented and decontextualized aspects of playlists.[52]

Alongside radio, we might cite educational, "relaxing classical," or "best of" compilation albums as precedents for themed playlists.[53] Streaming, though, has opened new possibilities through on-demand access, massive catalogs of music, the elimination of runtime constraints, the ability to update contents in real time, and increasingly granular themes. As a consequence, while there are historical precedents for classical music playlists across media and formats, streaming services' features and interfaces have a particular tendency toward atomized, functional, and passive listening.

This tendency is amplified by the changing nature of musical paratexts in the digital age. Eric Drott notes that streaming platforms strive for a minimalist, frictionless interface that facilitates continuous listening; this "requires stripping away much of the information and iconography that frames the musical experience," including liner notes, detailed credits, and album packaging.[54] Materials such as liner notes—in some ways descendants of program notes—have historically played an important role in the production of classical music recordings and speak to expectations about the way audiences engage with this music.[55] Symes claims that there is an implicit assumption that listeners to classical recordings, by and large, wish to be informed about the music and will sit down and listen to a recording as they would in a concert, in relative quietness, with their ears attuned to the musical structures identified and discussed in the notes. As such, the notes are designed to produce a more attentive and reflective mode of listening, one that might be at loggerheads with other, more diverting modes of listening, such as when music is employed to induce emotional solace or revelry.[56]

The lack of contextual materials on most major streaming services thus reinforces notions of "diverting" rather than "attentive and reflective" modes of listening.

Furthermore, the lack of high fidelity audio options on platforms like Spotify and YouTube Music can be off-putting for classical listeners accustomed to the higher audio quality available via physical media such as LPs and CDs.[57] Audiophile tendencies are not exclusive to classical music, but developments in fidelity—from the LP to the CD to Hi-Res digital downloads—have traditionally been associated with classical music.[58] As Simon Frith writes, "all hi-fi inventions... have been marketed, at first, on the assumption that the consumers most concerned about sound quality and a permanent record library are 'serious' consumers, consuming 'serious' music."[59] It is no surprise, then, that many classical music listeners are dissatisfied with "lossy" (i.e., compressed) formats like MP3, Ogg Vorbis, and AAC.[60]

In response to the perceived misalignment between major streaming platforms and classical listening ideals, classical services like Apple Music Classical, Idagio, and Presto Music orient much of their marketing materials and platform design around attentive listening. Many promote their superior audio quality and have formed partnerships with hi-fi companies, strategically appealing to audiophiles.[61] Apple's press release for the launch of Apple Music Classical, for example, advertised that

Apple Music Classical features lossless audio of up to 24 bit/192 kHz throughout the service so listeners can experience the nuances of every performance. In Hi-Res Lossless mode, sound is so astoundingly crisp and clear that each note feels close enough to touch. And thanks to revolutionary Spatial Audio with Dolby Atmos, listeners can enjoy thousands of recordings from the best seat in the concert hall, immersed in a 360-degree soundscape where music comes from every direction, including above.[62]

This statement echoes Legge's comment from decades earlier that a classical recording should impart the feeling of being "in the best seat in an acoustically perfect hall," reifying beliefs about what constitutes proper listening (and the cultural capital that accompanies it).

In addition to improved audio quality, a unique aspect of classical listening behavior that many classical services address is the comparison of multiple recordings of a work. Unlike popular music, in which a recording is typically synonymous with the "text," canonical classical works are interpreted many times and can have upward of a thousand professional recordings by different performers, recording studios, and labels. Many classical listeners are not content with listening to only one version of a work, relishing the act of closely comparing the nuances of interpretation, performance, recording, and production. Niche classical services make comparative listening easier through improved browsing—such as having a metadata parameter for the "work," as discussed above—and some have even developed features for expressly this purpose: Tempso's "Compare" tool allows users to easily search multiple recordings of the same work, while Idagio's "Live Compare" feature lets listeners switch between multiple recordings of a work in real time (though this feature is only available for a handful of works).

Another important differentiation strategy for classical services is providing additional context for recordings through detailed album credits, digital liner notes, and editorial content such as reviews, interviews, features, recommendations, biographies, historical context, genre descriptions, and educational materials. Presto Music's CEO, Chris O'Reilly, tells me that the company's editorial content has been key to its success:

- It's a very important part of what we do. We see ourselves as a cross between a retailer and a magazine that's helping people find the good stuff.... When we write about a new recording and explain why this is something we think people should listen to, we sell loads of it. And that happens because they've developed a trust for what we write about and they trust our team. Our strategy has always been to include all that within the streaming service as well, and it would be that which would differentiate our streaming service from other streaming services out there.[63]

Streaming also opens up new formats for editorial content and curation. For example, Grammofy interspersed music with optional explanatory podcasts—a feature that Krohn-Grimberghe describes as bridging radio with streaming $^{64}$ —and Apple Music Classical has several "Track by Track" albums where the featured artist discusses each piece on the album. These practices resemble the commentary of a radio host but are novel in the flexibility they afford listeners.

Educational materials are a particularly useful way in which some classical streaming services can distinguish themselves from mainstream platforms. Classical music is a genre with high barriers to entry and can be inaccessible on streaming services without guidance and context (in part as a consequence of metadata issues). Prior to being acquired by Apple, Primephonic had especially robust educational content. The service's "Maestro" listening guides led listeners through pieces in real time, providing short descriptions throughout the recording that analyzed musical features and provided historical context.[65] Additionally, Primephonic featured a podcast series narrated by its Head of Curation, Guy Jones, to introduce users to the history of classical music along with its most famous works. (This has been adapted to Apple Music Classical as the nine-part podcast series *The Story of Classical*, also narrated by Jones, who is now the Global Head of Classical Editorial at Apple.) Primephonic combined some of these educational features on "Ludwig," a ten-week classical crash course curated by Jones that combined podcasts, email lessons, and introductory playlists. Marketing the launch of the course, Steffens stated that to ensure the genre's survival amidst the digital revolution, we also need to make it more accessible for those with less knowledge about the genre, especially since the education system has dramatically lowered coverage of classical music in its curriculums. The launch of Ludwig is, therefore, the next step in our mission to create a better future for classical music. From the very beginning, we have worked tirelessly to make a genre traditionally associated with elitism more accessible to the masses.[66]

While education is framed in both ethical and existential terms here, it was also a tactic for user acquisition and retention. After all, if niche classical services fail to acquire new users, they will not survive.

The attempt to make classical music more accessible also explains why many classical services do not shy away from the passive listening features of mainstream platforms. Indeed, even as these services seek to accommodate attentive listening practices, they also adopt passive features like contextual playlists. Apple Music Classical, Idagio, and (formerly) Primephonic all feature countless themed classical music playlists similar to those on Spotify, although typically with a greater emphasis on human curators than on algorithms.[67] In some cases, classical services have introduced features specifically designed for passive listening: Primephonic's radio tool, seen in figure 4, allowed users to create an algorithmically generated playlist based on customizable parameters, while Idagio's mood wheel, seen in figure 5, is a drag-and-drop feature that randomly selects classical pieces according to the chosen mood.[68] Given that features such as mood-based playlists and algorithmic recommendations are increasingly used to access and discover music on major streaming services, it makes sense that companies such as Idagio and Primephonic would implement passive listening options even if they do not align with stereotypical classical listening habits.[69]

As this range of features indicates, many classical services cater to multiple types of listener, from serious and attentive to casual and passive. Steffens explains that Primephonic focused on serving three listener "archetypes": experts who know exactly what they want to listen to, consumers familiar with classical music but eager to explore more, and beginners who need guidance. He claims that the platform's mood-based playlists and radio tool were designed to aid newcomers, arguing that these features gave listeners an entry point into what is often seen as an intimidating genre.[70] Similarly, when discussing Idagio's mood wheel, Janczukowicz indicated that "we wanted a platform that suits both specialists and the huge number of people who say 'well I really don't have a clue about classical music, but I kind of like it and would like to dig deeper into this world."71 He asserts that even classical music experts use the feature, speaking to the multiplicity of listening behaviors demonstrated by individual consumers: the same person may want to listen to classical music intently in one instance and have it on in the background in another. How and why people actually listen to classical music and engage with these platforms in their daily lives does not necessarily correspond to the views of CEOs and programmers, but the statements of industry professionals nevertheless reveal how assumptions about listening habits are embedded into platform design and may, in turn, shape user behavior.

Appealing to different listening modalities is strategic from a business perspective, allowing companies that are marketing to a relatively niche audience to expand their reach and relevance. A 2019 report on the state of classical music streaming conducted by the research firm MIDiA (sponsored, tellingly, by Idagio) distinguishes between two categories of classical listener: “aficionados” (experts for whom classical music is important) and “enthusiasts” (those who like classical music casually).[72] The report states that “Classical enthusiasts are a large addressable base of consumers while

Classical aficionados are smaller in number but highly valuable.[73] Rob Overman, founder and CEO of the classical video streaming service Symphony/live, refers to the MIDiA study as a "key report" and notes that research conducted by his company yielded similar results. He tells me that the classical enthusiastic audience is a large potential market and that "this is an enormous cake that can be divided between many services that have the right model."[74]

While the companies that sponsor research on classical music streaming clearly have a vested interest in the results—including demonstrating the potential growth and value of such services to investors—the idea of marketing classical music to multiple audiences predates the streaming era by several decades, if not centuries. Marius Carboni notes that, from the 1980s, “twofold, or tiered, operations emerged: ‘core’ classical, targeting the more knowledgeable classical consumer; and ‘strategic’ classical, creating projects to reach a consumer base not necessarily as interested in, say, the intricacies of musical interpretation.”[75] This phenomenon can be traced further back to “serious” and “mass” classical radio stations established in the early twentieth century, or even to the Kenner-Liebhaber (expert-amateur) dichotomy theorized in eighteenth-century Germany.[76] In other words, the distinction between the enthusiast and the aficionado—and the sense that both audiences should be addressed—has been around for a very long time, even if not expressed in precisely these terms.

In the streaming era, however, the emphasis has shifted away from marketing different musical content to these audiences (for example, targeting symphonic music to aficionados and classical crossover to enthusiasts) toward promoting the highly personalized, data-driven features of digital platforms. Options like mood-based playlists and algorithmic recommendations targeting enthusiasts may very well provide an effective introduction to classical music that could dismantle some of the genre's elitist underpinnings. But these features also clearly function as part of a larger business model strategically designed to attract paying subscribers and maintain their engagement.

Although classical music initially suffered in the transition to streaming, recent reports suggest that the rate of classical streaming is now increasing.[77] This is likely due in part to the accessibility of classical music on streaming services, the proliferation of contextual playlists (and especially of piano music used for focusing and relaxing), and listeners' increasingly diverse tastes.[78] Dickon Stainer, president and CEO of Universal Music Group's Global Classics & Jazz division, states that "classical is now incredibly broad and our job really is to serve the specialist and generalist classical consumer.... Such is the breadth of [Universal's classical] labels now that they're attracting far younger consumers, and streaming has been a huge area of access for people."[79] Furthermore, while classical music listeners are often stereotyped as older and less tech-savvy or media literate, some research shows an increasing willingness among audiences of all ages to stream music.[80]

All of this reveals that there is a tangible relationship between classical music listening practices, cultural attitudes toward classical music, and the business of streaming. It is worth stressing that this relationship is mutual: streaming services and their affordances may shape the way many people listen to and think about classical music, but, at the same time, these services are often designed around the (real or perceived) desires of users. Though analyzing platforms and their creators' perspectives has limitations for understanding how people actually use these services, it speaks to the way media can reflect, reify, and redefine assumptions about listenership. Just as the concert hall, phonograph, and radio influenced—and were influenced by—classical music culture and listening practices, so streaming services mediate how people experience classical music in the twenty-first century.

# Streaming Royalties and Revenue Streams

Streaming also has implications for the ways in which classical music performers, composers, and labels distribute and monetize their recordings. One of the most serious critiques of streaming in both popular and scholarly discourse has been the poor treatment of artists. Most streaming services use a pro rata royalty model that pools revenue from all subscribers and then distributes payments to rightsholders according to the total proportion of streams they have received.[81] This system has been widely criticized for perpetuating historic inequities toward artists, resulting in extremely low payments, a lack of transparency, and winnertakes-all dynamics.[82]

Classical music is no exception to these concerns. In 2015 journalist Charlotte Gardner described how the streaming model has been detrimental for many independent classical music record labels, including Hyperion Records, Delphian Records, and Gimell. She quoted Hyperion's CEO Simon Perry:

If in the future there are no CD sales, no downloads and people only wish to stream, then how on earth does the label find the £1.5 million in cash to pay for the 75 recordings we make each year?... I don't have a pot of gold at the bottom of my garden. If we then start offering our recordings on streaming services for virtually no financial return, and the majority of people decide that that's how they wish to consume music in the future, then I can't see where the income will come from to pay for all the new recordings.[83]

Similarly, interviews conducted by Carboni reveal that many independent classical labels are suffering in the transition to a streaming-dominant music industry.[84] Both Gardner and Carboni note that, while some classical music industry personnel acknowledge streaming's potential benefits and the need to adapt to new technologies, many see it as a threat.

While there were still numerous streaming holdouts in the mid-2010s, it is increasingly difficult for independent record labels and artists to exist outside of the streaming ecosystem. As Delphian's co-founder, Paul Baxter, commented in 2021,

We get paid between 0.002 and 0.006 pence per stream and if it's on YouTube, you can add another decimal point. So we can't pass on royalties to artists if there are none, it's frightening.... Our artists have to appear on playlists and expect as wide a digital distribution as possible so we have no choice but to accept the terms of the streaming services.[85]

Hyperion initially withheld its catalog from streaming services, but acquiesced after being acquired by the major record label Universal Music Group in 2023.[86] Some classical musicians praise the marketing potential and accessibility that streaming provides—Apple Music Classical, for instance, strategically gathered quotations from famous classical musicians such as Yo-Yo Ma and Hilary Hahn that testified to the quality of the service[87]—but many still lament the paltry payouts.

Streaming's royalty model has been criticized across genres, but classical music is disproportionately disadvantaged by the current system. Because the pro rata model calculates payments on the basis of the total proportion of streams an artist's music receives, a single stream of a two-minute pop song generates as much revenue as a twenty-minute symphony movement. Since the average classical piece is longer than the average piece of popular music, classical music will, on average, make less money through royalties.[88] This inequity is compounded by the difficulties some users have in discovering classical music (partly as a result of metadata limitations), by the fact that many classical listeners will not have music playing continuously in the background over long stretches of time (reducing the total number of streams), and by the existence of hundreds of recordings of the most famous classical works, all competing to be heard (with streaming services foregrounding only the most popular).

The stream-based payout model leads to unjust payments even within the genre of classical music. O'Reilly is adamant that the industry should switch to a new royalty model:

- I think that's the big problem. You can listen to Bach's Goldberg Variations and it's thirty-two tracks in an hour, and a Mahler symphony is four tracks in an hour. You're saying that Bach is worth eight times Mahler and it should be paid eight times as much? ... When the market is more streaming everywhere, and CDs and downloads are even smaller, I don't know how a label could afford to record big works with long movements if the model is as it currently is across the major services.[89]

This not only points to a striking disparity in the way music is remunerated within the classical genre on streaming services, but also to how labels' output and artists' creative practices may change in response. O'Reilly cautions that some labels have already started separating long works into several shorter tracks to generate more royalties.[90] As one extreme example, in early 2025 the classical record label Erato (owned by Warner Music Group) released an album of pianist Stephen Hough performing Mozart's Piano Concertos nos. 9 and 29, splitting these works into dozens of tracks, each around one minute long.[91]

Concerns about classical recording revenue are not new to streaming. Revenue from classical recordings has long paled in comparison to that garnered from live performances. The cost of recording classical music—particularly operatic and orchestral works with large ensembles—often far exceeds the returns. Indeed, classical recordings are typically financed not only by record labels, but by patrons, government grants, and sometimes even musicians themselves.[92] Recordings nevertheless remain an essential marketing and branding tool for composers, performing organizations, and labels. The reduced revenue from streaming exacerbates financial precarity for classical artists, making it increasingly difficult to sustain high-quality recordings and a livelihood.

To improve income for artists, many classical streaming services have implemented new royalty models designed to accommodate classical music and its listening practices. Grammofy, Idagio, Presto Music, and Primephonic all negotiated per-second royalty rates with record labels in which revenue is calculated on the basis of a track's duration, rather than the number of times it is streamed.[93] O'Reilly says, "I think there's a genuine desire amongst all labels, including the majors, to try and make remuneration fair and get the right recordings the right amount of revenue. So I found that easier than I feared it might [be], to be honest, those negotiations.[94] This model has yet to be taken up by any of the major streaming services, however, and Apple Music Classical seemingly did not adopt the per-second royalty model when it acquired Primephonic—despite Steffens's own protestations against the prevailing royalty model during his time at Primephonic.[95] Furthermore, while the shift to per-second payouts is arguably a more equitable way of distributing revenue to rightholders, it does not actually increase the pool of money being distributed. Companies rarely acknowledge this reality when touting new royalty models and thus might be seen as obscuring root problems while leveraging supposedly ethical behavior to attract customers (even if they also earnestly believe in supporting classical artists).

Some classical streaming services have created new revenue streams for artists. Apple Music Classical, for instance, established partnerships with several prominent classical orchestras and musicians, leading to exclusive recordings and the "Track by Track" albums mentioned above. Idagio, for its part, offers exclusive livestreamed concerts, an "Academy" where users can take lessons with teaching artists, and masterclasses and workshops held by notable classical musicians, pedagogues, and industry personnel.[96] Janczukowicz tells me that "the reason I founded Idagio is to do something for artists and audiences. And the question is what can you do for artists? . . . I think what you have to do is figure out what are the possibilities for classical artists to earn money online. And you cannot do it with a recording, but you can do it with courses for example, with curation."[97]

Although niche classical services are attempting to benefit the classical music business and artists' livelihoods via alternative royalty models and new revenue streams, it is unclear how sustainable these efforts will be when constrained by limited userbases and financial resources. This is particularly true when major streaming services and record labels continue to operate under models that do not effectively support classical musicians. Krohn-Grimberghe comments,

- I know so many classical musicians, and I know how little they make from streaming. So do many other musicians, so it's not just classical music. But in that corner that we were in we really wanted to make a difference and prove that there is a different way to do it where you can make money for everybody. I still believe that is, in theory, possible. But in practice, if there is so much power by three or four companies, there's very little you can do.[98]

As Krohn-Grimberghe implies, the power structures of the music industry make it challenging to enact change on a wider scale without systemic reforms.

# Coda: Platforms and Power

Apple's acquisition of Primephonic came as a surprise to me. The deal was announced just two weeks after I last spoke with Steffens, who (understandably) did not mention the impending deal during our interview. When I contacted him afterward to see if he could put me in touch with another member of the Primephonic team, he politely informed me that he could no longer be of help since Apple has strict regulations about sharing information externally. This response was to be expected—major tech companies like Apple are notorious for their strict non-disclosure agreements—but it reflects how access can become restricted when small-scale start-ups go mainstream.[99] The acquisition also raises questions about the viability of niche streaming services and the power that major platforms have over the music industry and musical culture.

Primephonic's executives were transparent about their hopes for acquisition from the outset. In a 2019 interview with *PC Magazine*, Steffens acknowledged that "I think it would be best [for] classical music if we were to get acquired... because our technology [would] have a much larger audience than we probably ever could."100 When the company was acquired by Apple, he claimed that

bringing the best of Primephonic to Apple Music subscribers is a tremendous development for the classical music industry.... Artists love the Primephonic service and what we've done in classical, and now we have the ability to join with Apple to deliver the absolute best experience to millions of listeners. We get to bring classical music to the mainstream and connect a new generation of musicians with the next generation of audience.[101]

In using this language, Steffens presents Apple Music Classical as classical music's savior, a bulwark against the genre's digital demise.

It is true that Apple's significant resources and brand name can potentially help classical music on a larger scale, especially since the tens of millions of Apple Music subscribers can access the classical music service at no extra cost. At the same time, however, Apple's acquisition of Primephonic also means that power over this music is further concentrated in the hands of a massive tech company whose stakes in music lie mostly in its ability to promote other products and services.[102] From music critic Alex Ross's perspective, "Apple is a scarcely powerful economic colossus, dependent on global networks of labor exploitation, and its approach to classical music seems designed more to subsume the art form than to serve it on its own terms."[103] In September 2023, Apple even acquired the classical label BIS Records, further expanding its ownership, control, and cultural capital in the field of classical music.[104] Scholars and music industry reporters alike have pointed out the danger of concentrating market power in just a handful of companies and their platforms, which then have tremendous influence over what music people listen to and how rightsholders get remunerated.[105]

In some ways, smaller-scale classical services are better positioned to support classical music and musicians. They are not beholden to major tech companies and therefore have more control over how they run their businesses. But niche services face challenges of their own: they target a small subset of listeners, which limits their ability to scale, and their survival is often contingent on raising millions of dollars in venture capital to pay for building the platform and licensing music.[106] The classical market is relatively small, and most people—even ardent fans—are not willing to pay for multiple streaming subscriptions, with the result that it can be extremely challenging to establish a sufficient paying userbase. Considering that even major streaming services with tens of millions of subscribers are rarely profitable, niche services are clearly in a precarious position.

Describing his time at Grammofy, Krohn-Grimberghe recalls that the company needed to raise millions of dollars for up-front payments to record labels, when their user numbers did not support that kind of growth:

You have to spend a couple of million just to operate in a market where you have zero customers yet and where you know you will not be able to recoup that for many, many years. Add to that the marketing you would need to get all these listeners in. And then you realize that there aren't that many listeners that are willing to pay for a stand-alone classical streaming service. We realized that it just wouldn't work.[107]

Krohn-Grimberghe says he was aware from the start that there would need to be systemic change in order for niche services like Grammofy to be viable, but thought it would be possible to demonstrate in negotiations with record labels that it could be beneficial for all parties. He claims that independent labels agreed, but that major labels could not or would not change their licensing deals.

Reflecting on the viability of niche classical services in 2020, Krohn-Grimberghe commented that [Idagio and Primephonic] are far from being profitable and their operational costs, marketing spending, and respective conversions to paying subscribers are in line with the projections the Grammofy team calculated. Again, like the other services before them, these two will either fail, continue to burn through investor money, or begin to succeed when the system gives, the rules change, and the labels and services actually work together on different models for different services that work with different audiences and solve genre-specific issues.[108]

These comments predate Apple's acquisition of Primephonic, and yet since that time there has been no meaningful systemic change in the music industry's power dynamics.

Some classical services have found ways around relying on external investors. As a previously established music retailer, Presto Music was able to leverage its long-standing relationships with record labels to secure licensing deals that would otherwise be cost prohibitive for start-ups. As O'Reilly explains,

We certainly were in a unique position to do this because of our background in CDs and downloads. I think if you were a new service that came along and wanted to license content from the major labels, you would likely be looking at a hefty advance and high minimum guarantees. But the fact that we've got this twenty-year history of running a successful business, and engaging customers in classical music... meant we were able to navigate some of those discussions in a way that a new service would find more challenging: they'd have to put a big chunk of money on the table, they'd need big investors for that.[109]

Tempso, on the other hand, avoids the extensive licensing costs by layering on top of existing streaming services using their API (application programming interface). Yet even in cases like Presto Music and Tempso, where there is no reliance on external investors, there is still steep competition and a limited potential userbase that is only reachable via extensive and costly marketing efforts. It is, after all, difficult to compete with a company like Apple.

---

The business and culture of classical music have long been bound to technological advances and broader music industry developments. Throughout the twentieth century, classical music and its audiences shaped, and were shaped by, new distribution methods (e.g., radio, TV, and the internet) and new recording formats (e.g., LP, CD, and MP3). Now, in the twenty-first century, the genre's cultural reach and economic potential are being influenced by digital platforms like music streaming services.

As I have argued, the effects of streaming on classical music are complex and multifaceted. In many ways, the genre has never been as accessible as in the streaming era: listeners no longer need to purchase individual recordings and can stream an extensive collection of classical music for a relatively low cost. But as evidenced by major platforms' metadata frameworks, features, and royalty models, most streaming services prioritize popular music, posing significant challenges for browsing, listening to, and earning money from classical recordings. Even when dedicated classical services are specifically designed to accommodate classical music listeners and artists, they struggle to achieve the same reach and accrue the same resources as mainstream platforms. Although streaming is unlikely to threaten classical music's survival—the "death of classical music" is now a well-worn trope—the future of classical music will undoubtedly be tied to the digital platforms through which it is increasingly circulated, consumed, and controlled.

# ABSTRACT

This article investigates how streaming platforms are affecting classical music circulation and consumption. It demonstrates that mainstream services such as Spotify are not built with classical music in mind, largely because their metadata frameworks and listening features—including playlists and algorithmic recommenders—are ineffective for the genre. Not only do such frameworks and features provide incomplete or inaccurate information, but they also facilitate passive listening experiences seemingly at odds with classical music's historical emphasis on active listening, while offering little guidance for new listeners. Furthermore, most streaming services' royalty models disproportionately disadvantage classical artists. Dedicated classical music streaming services like Idagio, Presto Music, and Apple Music Classical attempt to address these issues through proprietary metadata databases, active listening features, and royalty models designed to support classical artists. However, current power dynamics within the music industry ensure that digital platforms and tech companies increasingly control how people access, listen to, and make money from classical music in the twenty-first century. Through analysis of platform design, marketing materials, and interviews conducted with the CEOs of classical music streaming services, this article argues that streaming is changing the culture and business of classical music, participating in a longer history of classical music's relationship with new media and the music industry.
