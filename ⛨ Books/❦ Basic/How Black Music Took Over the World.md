> MELVIN GIBBS

# Ⅰ BARETTA 
**巴雷塔**

Guppies started my education in the science of Black music.

> Guppies 开启了我对黑人音乐科学的启蒙教育。

I was walking home, sidewalk in front of me, freshly trimmed hedges to the left of me, parked cars to the right of me, carrying a few of the fish in a small plastic bag. Sparks of light bounced off of the water as the bag glimmered in the sun.

> 我正步行回家，面前是人行道，左边是刚修剪过的树篱，右边是停放的车辆，手里拎着一个小塑料袋，里面装着几条孔雀鱼。袋子在阳光下闪闪发光，水面上跳跃着点点光芒。

As I pulled up in front of the apartment building I lived in, swiveling left so I could go inside, I saw the man all the teens thought was the coolest in the neighborhood opening one of the two glass doors at the entrance.

> 我在所住的公寓楼前停下，向左转身准备进门时，看到那个被所有青少年视为街区最酷的男人正在推开入口处的两扇玻璃门之一。

His government name was Joseph Daddiego. His wife called him Joe. His friends and colleagues called him Papo. But when we talked among ourselves, the kids in the neighborhood called him Baretta.

> 他的官方名字是 Joseph Daddiego 。他的妻子叫他 Joe 。他的朋友和同事叫他 Papo 。但当我们私下聊天时，街坊邻居的孩子们都叫他 Baretta 。

Baretta was the name of the lead character on one of the most popular shows on television in the '70s. In it, actor Robert Blake played a disguise-wearing, crime-solving detective who lived in a hotel with a cockatoo as his roommate. Like Baretta, Papo had a cockatoo. He would walk around with the bird perched on his shoulder. He wasn't a cop and didn't wear disguises, but Papo was notorious in my neighborhood because he kept exotic pets. For a while, he had a spider monkey whose literal monkey bars were a T-shaped wooden contraption the size of a middle schooler. From time to time he would bring the monkey outside directly in front of the building we lived in to give it more space, to the delight of all the kids on the block.

> Baretta 是 70 年代最受欢迎的电视节目之一中主角的名字。剧中，演员 Robert Blake 扮演一位擅长伪装、侦破案件的侦探，他与一只凤头鹦鹉同住在一家旅馆里。和 Baretta 一样，Papo 也养了一只凤头鹦鹉。他经常让那只鸟站在他的肩膀上四处走动。他既不是警察，也不伪装自己，但 Papo 在我们街区臭名昭著，因为他养了一些奇异的宠物。有一段时间，他养了一只蜘蛛猴，而那只猴子的专属“猴架”是一个 T 形木制装置，大小和一个中学生差不多。他时不时会把猴子带到我们住的楼前，给它更多活动空间，这让街区的孩子们都欣喜不已。

But that day it was all about guppies. Noticing the plastic bag in my hand, he said to me, "You keep fish." I nodded my head and said, "Yep." He replied, "Well, come here then" and turned around and went back into the building. Mindful of the fish swimming in the water sloshing around in the bag in my hand—there was just enough water in the bags to ensure the fish would survive the four-block journey from the pet store to the tank in my parents' living room—I followed him inside and up the stairs to the third floor.

> 但那天的主角是孔雀鱼。注意到我手里的塑料袋，他对我说：“你养鱼啊。”我点点头说：“嗯。”他回答：“那好，过来吧。”然后转身走回了楼里。我留意着袋子里晃荡的水中游动的鱼——袋子里的水刚好够让鱼从宠物店到父母家客厅鱼缸的四条街路程中存活——跟着他进去，上了三楼。

Papo lived in the apartment next to my family's. Though at that point he had been living next door to us for many months, this was my first visit. Entering his home was like entering an apartment-sized combination of the New York Aquarium and Brooklyn's Prospect Park Zoo. It was filled with fish tanks. There were lines of rising air bubbles, undulating fish, and swaying aquatic plants everywhere. His cockatoo's lair was a section of his living room. The bird, happily perched inside his cage, alternated between directing some of his repertoire of sentence fragments at me and picking at pumpkin seeds sitting in a feeder. The cage door was open, so the bird could enter and leave as it pleased. I was mesmerized by what I saw.

> Papo 住在我家隔壁的公寓里。尽管那时他已经在我们隔壁住了好几个月，但这还是我第一次拜访。走进他家，就像走进了一个公寓大小的 New York Aquarium 和 Brooklyn's Prospect Park Zoo 的结合体。里面摆满了鱼缸。到处都是一串串上升的气泡、游动的鱼和摇曳的水生植物。他的凤头鹦鹉的巢穴占据了他客厅的一部分。那只鸟快乐地栖息在笼子里，时而向我展示它的一些句子片段，时而去啄食喂食器里的南瓜籽。笼门是开着的，所以鸟儿可以随意进出。我被眼前的景象迷住了。

Papo told me that the reason he had so many tanks was that he bred fish to sell to pet shops. I told him I'd been breeding guppies in my ten-gallon tank next door, adding that I'd bought the ones I was carrying for just that purpose. Papo absorbed this info, then made an ask: "Do you think your father would give us some space in the basement for some fish tanks?"

> Papo 告诉我，他之所以有这么多鱼缸，是因为他养鱼卖给宠物店。我告诉他，我一直在隔壁的十加仑鱼缸里养孔雀鱼，并补充说我买这些鱼就是为了这个目的。Papo 吸收了这些信息，然后问道：“你觉得你父亲能在地下室给我们腾出一些空间放鱼缸吗？”

My father was the superintendent of the building. He was responsible for, among many other things, the care of the basement. Because of that, I, his oldest son and number-one helper, had full access to the building's underground spaces. That access gave me the clout to upgrade Papo's hustle as well as my own. With my father's blessing, we set up a battery of tanks in the basement. Soon after, with my younger brother Marvin, and me, as his junior partners, Papo expanded to serve pet shops throughout Brooklyn.

> 我父亲是大楼的管理员。他负责，除了许多其他事情之外，地下室的维护。正因如此，我，作为他的长子兼头号助手，可以完全进入大楼的地下空间。这种权限让我有能力提升 Papo 的生意以及我自己的生意。在我父亲的许可下，我们在地下室设置了一组鱼缸。不久之后，Papo 扩展业务，服务整个布鲁克林的宠物店，而我的弟弟 Marvin 和我作为他的初级合伙人。

Each time I entered Papo's apartment I got more used to it, so I began to absorb the energy and climate of Papo's world—to learn from it. As time passed, I was able to see beyond the level of his zoological hustle and notice the parallel track in his life. Alongside the fish, birds, and mammals, I noticed objects made of wood and metal. They looked like things I'd seen in the window of the music store I'd walk by on Prospect Avenue in The Bronx when I traveled uptown to visit my aunt. That store catered to the Boricuas, who, having moved to the South Bronx from Puerto Rico, shared the immediate area with a Black population mostly made up of people who'd fled various Southern states. The two communities created a cultural mosaic whose inhabitants both defended and blurred the boundaries between the two. I never asked Papo if he was Puerto Rican directly. He had a Spanish nickname and carried himself in a way I recognized, so I left it at that.

> 每次我走进 Papo 的公寓，都会更加习惯这里的环境，于是我开始吸收 Papo 世界的能量和氛围——并从中学习。随着时间的推移，我逐渐看透了他那些动物生意的表面，注意到他生活中并行不悖的另一条轨迹。除了鱼、鸟和哺乳动物，我还注意到一些木制和金属的物件。它们看起来就像我在布朗克斯区 Prospect Avenue 上那家音乐商店橱窗里见过的东西——那是我去上城拜访姑妈时路过的地方。那家店主要服务波多黎各人，他们从波多黎各搬到南布朗克斯，与主要由逃离美国南方各州的黑人群体共同生活在这片区域。这两个社群形成了一幅文化马赛克，其中的居民既捍卫又模糊着彼此之间的界限。我从未直接问过 Papo 他是否是波多黎各人。他有一个西班牙语昵称，举止也让我感到熟悉，于是我便不再追问。

Papo Daddiego had the largest record collection I'd seen inside a place where someone lived. I had pretty eclectic musical taste for a teenager. I was listening to John Coltrane and Herbie Hancock alongside my Kool & the Gang and Jimi Hendrix records. And I'd already noticed the similarities between Miles Davis's On the Corner album and King Tubby's dub. But a lot of Papo's records didn't look like the records I, or my friends, or their parents, or my grown relatives owned. Instead of a band name and an album's title, the record would just have "Music of" and the name of a country written on it.

> Papo Daddiego 拥有我见过的最大的唱片收藏，这些唱片存放在有人居住的地方。作为一个青少年，我的音乐品味相当广泛。我听 John Coltrane 和 Herbie Hancock，同时也听 Kool & the Gang 和 Jimi Hendrix 的唱片。而且我已经注意到 Miles Davis 的专辑《On the Corner》与 King Tubby 的 dub 音乐之间的相似之处。但 Papo 的许多唱片看起来不像我、我的朋友、他们的父母或我的成年亲戚拥有的唱片。唱片上不是乐队名和专辑名，而是写着“Music of”和一个国家的名字。

Papo knew I was into music. I'd set up a four-channel listening system in my living room and had mounted two of the four speakers I had on the wall that separated my parents' apartment from Papo's. So he'd inevitably hear whatever I was listening to. That must've been why he decided that I was ready the day he pulled a record that had "Music of Cameroon" written on it off his shelf and placed it on the turntable. I was ready for it, but just barely. That music was like nothing I had heard before but also like everything I'd heard before. The texture of the sound, which was based on hand drums and singing, sort of reminded me of the Latin music I'd hear in The Bronx. The layers of rhythms I heard in the music sounded kind of like a sideways version of James Brown's funk to my ears. But as I listened to that music in its totality, what was coming out of Papo's speakers overran those markers, and that flood of music made its own space inside my psyche.

> Papo 知道我喜欢音乐。我在客厅里搭建了一个四声道聆听系统，并将四个音箱中的两个挂在分隔父母公寓与 Papo 公寓的墙上。所以，无论我在听什么，他都不可避免地会听到。这一定是他决定让我“准备好”的原因——那天，他从架子上抽出一张写着“Music of Cameroon”的唱片，放到唱机上。我确实准备好了，但只是勉强。那音乐既是我从未听过的，又仿佛我听过的一切。基于手鼓和演唱的声音质地，让我隐约想起在 Bronx 听到的拉丁音乐。音乐中的层层节奏，在我听来有点像 James Brown 的放克乐的侧向变体。但当我完整地聆听那音乐时，从 Papo 音箱里涌出的声音超越了那些参照，那股音乐的洪流在我内心开辟了自己的空间。

After that turning point, Papo began to introduce me to his other world. In this world, objects fashioned from plants, metal, and animal parts become portals to a realm where sounds become vehicles for human movement powered by rhythm. Seed pods, sculpted metal, gourds, goat skins—all were carriers of sound, messengers from the world of rhythm. Papo told me that the reason the music of Cameroon reminded me of Latin music was because salsa music, the music that the Puerto Ricans of New York loved, had, like the blues my aunt Rachel who lived in The Bronx loved, originally came from Africa. The sounds and rhythms of African music were the parents of Latin music. Similarly, the sounds of the different percussion instruments in his house were the children of sounds that came from Africa. The technique, the rhythms, the way they were played—that came from Africa as well.
Soon after, he introduced me to all the musical instruments in his possession, big, medium, and small. Papo demonstrated the extensive range of sounds a shekere (pronounced shek-air-ray), an instrument made out of a gourd covered with a net made of beads, could create. He introduced me to the hypnotic sounds of an instrument from Brazil that looked like a bow called a berimbau (pronounced bear-rim-baow). In short Papo gave me a comprehensive introduction to traditional African music and some of its children in the West.

> 在那个转折点之后，Papo 开始向我介绍他的另一个世界。在这个世界里，由植物、金属和动物部位制成的物体成为通往一个领域的门户，在这个领域中，声音成为由节奏驱动的人类运动的载体。种子荚、雕刻的金属、葫芦、山羊皮——都是声音的载体，来自节奏世界的信使。Papo 告诉我，喀麦隆音乐让我想起拉丁音乐的原因是因为萨尔萨音乐——纽约的波多黎各人喜爱的音乐——和住在 the Bronx 的我的姑妈 Rachel 喜爱的布鲁斯音乐一样，最初都来自非洲。非洲音乐的声音和节奏是拉丁音乐的父母。同样，他家中不同打击乐器的声音是来自非洲的声音的子女。技巧、节奏、演奏方式——也都来自非洲。不久之后，他向我介绍了他拥有的所有乐器，大的、中的和小的。Papo 演示了 shekere（发音为 shek-air-ray）——一种由葫芦制成的乐器，上面覆盖着珠子制成的网——所能产生的广泛声音范围。他向我介绍了一种来自巴西的乐器，它看起来像一把弓，叫做 berimbau（发音为 bear-rim-baow），其声音令人着迷。简而言之，Papo 向我全面介绍了传统非洲音乐及其在西方的一些子女。

After he was done, a conga drum of his migrated from the apartment next door to my room, and my journey as a musician began. The conga drum was brought to what Europeans called the New World by people from the Congo in West Central Africa. It is one drum in a set of two or three drums played to motivate people to dance and sing. I already knew that each drum had its own particular part that repeated. And I knew that the combination of these repeating parts creates a sound that people like to dance to. I didn't really know how that worked. I didn't really understand how the pieces fit together. I just knew, when it is done right, people moved. Soon, I understood that when a group of drums plays together, they work kind of like a choir. Each drum has a range—low, medium, or high—and each drum plays a part in creating the musical sound. Like a vocal choir, the drum choir wouldn't sound quite right if one of the voices was missing and would sound off, dissonant, if one of the voices executed their part incorrectly. One significant difference between a drum choir and the vocal choirs I knew is that in vocal choirs, the higher voices were usually the featured instruments and soloists. In African drum choirs, on the other hand, the lowest voices are often the featured instruments and soloists.

> 在他做完之后，他的一面康加鼓从隔壁公寓搬到了我的房间，我作为音乐家的旅程就此开始。这种康加鼓是由来自中非西部的刚果人带到欧洲人所谓的新世界的。它是一套由两到三面鼓组成的鼓组中的一面，用来激励人们跳舞和唱歌。我已经知道每面鼓都有自己重复的特定部分。而且我知道，这些重复部分的组合创造出了一种人们喜欢随之起舞的声音。我并不真正知道那是如何运作的。我并不真正理解这些部分是如何组合在一起的。我只知道，当它处理得当时，人们就会动起来。很快，我明白了，当一组鼓一起演奏时，它们的作用有点像合唱团。每面鼓都有一个音域——低、中或高——每面鼓在创造音乐声音中都扮演一个角色。就像人声合唱团一样，如果缺少其中一个声部，鼓合唱团听起来就不太对劲；如果其中一个声部演奏错误，听起来就会走调、不和谐。鼓合唱团和我所知道的人声合唱团之间的一个显著区别是，在人声合唱团中，较高的声部通常是特色乐器和独奏者。另一方面，在非洲鼓合唱团中，最低的声部往往是特色乐器和独奏者。

Papo taught me that the salsa music Puerto Ricans in New York made and listened to is based on a kind of music called rumba (pronounced room-ba) that was developed by people of African descent in Cuba. Rumba is based on an older form of Afro-Cuban music called guaguancó (pronounced wah-wahn-ko). He emphasized that the root of what made people want to baile (dance) when they heard Latin music was the rhythm of guaguancó. My lessons began with that fundamental rhythm.
Commonly, all tall, single-head skinned drums made in the style of the drums used to play guaguancó, rumba, and salsa are called congas. But that's like calling every member of a singing group a voice. It tells you what kind of sound they're going to make but doesn't give you any idea of how that sound fits into the group. The drum Papo gave me was the drum that congeros—people who play conga drums—actually call a conga. It is the midrange drum in the ensemble. Its job is to set the rhythm. The low conga is called the tumba (toom-bah). It lays the foundation, anchoring the dancers and singers. The high drum, the flamboyant one, is called the quinto (keen-too). It's a show-off. It interjects when the singer is silent in the verses of the songs and improvises in concert with the dancers, both prodding them to show their best moves and sending out flourishes in response to the male dancers' moves.

> Papo 教给我，纽约的波多黎各人创作和聆听的萨尔萨音乐，是基于一种叫做 rumba（发音为 room-ba）的音乐，这种音乐是由古巴的非洲裔人民发展而来的。Rumba 又基于一种更古老的非洲-古巴音乐形式，叫做 guaguancó（发音为 wah-wahn-ko）。他强调，人们听到拉丁音乐时想要 baile（跳舞）的根源，正是 guaguancó 的节奏。我的课程就从这一基本节奏开始。通常，所有按照演奏 guaguancó、rumba 和 salsa 的鼓风格制作的高身、单面鼓，都被称为 congas。但这就像把歌唱组合的每个成员都叫做声音一样。它告诉你他们会发出什么样的声音，但完全没有说明这种声音在组合中如何融入。Papo 给我的鼓，正是 congeros（演奏康加鼓的人）实际称为 conga 的鼓。它是合奏中的中音鼓，任务是设定节奏。低音康加鼓叫做 tumba（toom-bah），它奠定基础，为舞者和歌手提供支撑。高音鼓，华丽的那一个，叫做 quinto（keen-too），它爱炫耀，在歌手于歌曲段落中沉默时插入，并与舞者即兴合奏，既激励他们展示最佳舞步，又对男舞者的动作做出华丽的回应。

As a beginner, my first mission was to keep a steady pace on the conga. Papo sat next to me playing the low and high drums while I learned to do so. Once I got comfortable playing my part, I was able to really listen, with my ears and my body, to the guaguancó rhythm. I could hear and feel how the part I was playing was both an echo of and response to the part the low drum was playing. I could hear the patterns embedded in the quinto's breaks. Its rhythmic juxtapositions started to make sense to me. Now, when I saw conga drummers playing in front of a building or in a playground in The Bronx, I could tell which ones were good. When I heard some good ones, I'd watch how they responded to the dancers who were always nearby, then go home and try to do what they did.

> 作为初学者，我的第一个任务是在康加鼓上保持稳定的节奏。Papo 坐在我旁边，敲击低音鼓和高音鼓，而我则学习这样做。一旦我熟悉了自己的部分，我就能用耳朵和身体真正聆听 guaguancó 节奏。我能听到并感觉到我演奏的部分既是低音鼓部分的回声，也是对其的回应。我能听到嵌入在 quinto 停顿中的模式。它的节奏并置开始变得有意义了。现在，当我在 The Bronx 看到康加鼓手在大楼前或操场上演奏时，我能分辨出哪些是好的。当我听到一些好的演奏时，我会观察他们如何回应附近总是存在的舞者，然后回家尝试模仿他们的做法。

Conga drummers started their playing off differently than the funk, gospel, and orchestral musicians I had seen. Instead of doing a "one, two, one two three four" vocal count off, or waving their hands or a baton, they clapped. Together they would clap a five-note rhythm—two times if there was a small group of people out in the streets with them and four times if there was a large one. Those additional sets of claps gave time for the audience to join the clapping—and the audience always did. To my Brooklyn-born eyes, it looked like every person in the South Bronx knew the routine.

> 康加鼓手开始演奏的方式，与我之前见过的放克、福音和管弦乐音乐家不同。他们不是用“一、二、一、二、三、四”的口头计数，也不是挥手或挥动指挥棒，而是拍手。他们会一起拍出一个五拍的节奏——如果街上跟着他们的人少，就拍两遍；如果人多，就拍四遍。这些额外的拍手给了观众加入拍手的时间——而观众总是会加入。以我布鲁克林出生的眼光来看，南布朗克斯的每个人似乎都知道这个套路。

***

Rhythms with five notes—or as I'll call percussive notes in this book, rhythms with five events—like the ones the congeros used to start their jams are a common feature of many forms of West African music and their descendants in the West. In those musics, patterns like the one the people in The Bronx clapped are played on percussion instruments pitched to cut through a dense sonic palette. In the Spanish-speaking countries of the Caribbean, they are often produced by striking two short wooden sticks together. In that part of the world, those five-note rhythmic patterns, and the instruments used to sound the patterns, are called claves (klah-veys).

> 由五个音符构成的节奏——或按我在本书中称之为打击乐音符的节奏，即五个事件的节奏——比如 congeros 用来开始他们的即兴演奏的那些节奏，是多种西非音乐及其在西方衍生物的共同特征。在这些音乐中，类似 The Bronx 的人们拍手的那种模式，是在音高足以穿透密集声景的打击乐器上演奏的。在加勒比海的西班牙语国家，它们通常通过敲击两根短木棍来产生。在世界那个地区，那些五音符的节奏模式，以及用于发出这些模式的乐器，被称为 claves (klah-veys)。

The word clave is usually translated as "key." That gives some sense of the rhythmic pattern's role, but clave has an array of meanings that include "code," "password," and most importantly for its musical function, "keystone." A keystone sits at the middle of an arch and locks the other pieces in place. That is an excellent analogy for the function of that five-note pattern in Spanish Afro-Caribbean music. The clave rhythm locks the other rhythms that appear in Latin music in place.
The rhythm most associated with clave is the keystone pattern used in the Afro-Cuban musical form called son montuno (sewn-mon-two-no). Son montuno is salsa's most direct musical parent. The form was shaped by the musical innovations of the Cuban composer Arsenio Rodríguez, whose work set the template Puerto Rican bandleaders in New York used. That canonical clave is a 3-2 clave. Salsa music is traditionally notated using what is called "cut time," which is a form of notation that divides a segment of time into eight equally spaced events. These groups of events are called bars or measures. Using clock notation to notate the grouping shows us that the 3-2 clave, the keystone pattern for salsa music, contains two alternating sets of rhythmic events.
The first measure contains three events (first diagram) and the second contains two (second diagram) (Figure 2).

The sets of rhythmic events that make up the clave are often reversed. When the two-event measure is played first, and the three-event measure follows, the pattern is called a 2-3 clave.

Instead of measure, I prefer to use the word cell, as in a biological one, that makes up the building blocks of earthly organisms. I'll define a cell as one measure of information coming from a particular instrument performing music that is based on, or derived from, African musical practice. Oxford defines organism as, among other things, "a whole with interdependent parts, likened to a living being."[^1-1] It's certainly valid to define performances by classic groups from the late twentieth century like the Fania All-Stars, Fela Kuti and Afrika 70, Parliament-Funkadelic, and ensembles led by John Coltrane, Sun Ra, and Bob Marley this way. I'd also say it's accurate to describe recordings by Jamaican dub pioneers Lee "Scratch" Perry and King Tubby, the Get Up with It–era recordings of jazz musician Miles Davis, the work the Bomb Squad did with rap group Public Enemy, and the music of UK drill group 67 this way as well.

> 与其使用“度量”这个词，我更倾向于用“细胞”一词，就像生物学中的细胞那样，它是构成地球生物体的基本单位。我将定义一个细胞为：来自特定乐器演奏的、基于或源自非洲音乐实践的一个信息度量单位。牛津词典将“有机体”定义为“一个由相互依赖的部分组成的整体，类似于生命体”[^1-1]。用这种方式来定义二十世纪末经典团体的表演无疑是合理的，例如 Fania All-Stars、Fela Kuti 与 Afrika 70、Parliament-Funkadelic，以及由 John Coltrane、Sun Ra 和 Bob Marley 领导的乐团。我同样认为，用这种方式来描述牙买加配音先驱 Lee "Scratch" Perry 和 King Tubby 的录音、爵士音乐家 Miles Davis 在《Get Up with It》时期的作品、Bomb Squad 与说唱团体 Public Enemy 的合作，以及英国 drill 团体 67 的音乐，也是准确的。

Musicologists and music teachers often speak of rhythmic cells and claves as if they are the same thing. They are not. The clave is the rhythmic figure "that locks the other pieces in place." It orients rhythms and sets their positions. In Cuban music and salsa, it guides by presenting the map the other instruments follow. But, as philosopher Alfred Korzybski said, "A map is not the territory." When the musical structure is completed, the guiding notes may be necessary to provide a satisfying musical structure. Or they may not. Rhythmic cells, on the other hand, are the territory. They are the rhythms the instruments and voices actually play or sing.

> 音乐学家和音乐教师经常把节奏细胞和 clave 当作同一回事，但它们并非如此。clave 是“锁定其他部分”的节奏型，它引导节奏并设定其位置。在古巴音乐和萨尔萨中，它通过呈现其他乐器遵循的图谱来引导。但正如哲学家 Alfred Korzybski 所说，“地图并非疆域。”当音乐结构完成时，引导音符可能对提供令人满意的音乐结构是必要的，也可能不是。而节奏细胞则是疆域，它们是乐器和人声实际演奏或演唱的节奏。

When conceptualizing the application and use of rhythmic cells in Black music, I find the word frame is more useful and accurate in practice than the word clave. Among its many definitions, a frame is "the underlying constructional system or structure that gives shape or strength."[^1-2] That is what rhythmic cells are and do in Black music. But frames also provide an "enclosing border."[^1-3] Add the word rhythmic to that, as in "an enclosing rhythmic border," and you have an accurate description of the function of clave in modern Afro-Cuban music and salsa.
Sometimes a rhythm is both a cell and a frame.

> 在构思黑人音乐中节奏细胞的应用与使用时，我发现 “frame”一词在实践上比 “clave”更为实用和准确。在其众多定义中，框架是 “赋予形状或强度的底层构造系统或结构”。[^1-2] 这正是节奏细胞在黑人音乐中的本质和作用。但框架也提供了 “一个封闭的边界”。[^1-3] 加上 “节奏” 一词，即 “一个封闭的节奏边界”，就能准确描述现代非裔古巴音乐和萨尔萨音乐中 clave 的功能。有时，一个节奏既是细胞也是框架。

Let's examine the 3-2 rhythmic frame more closely. Looking at the two cells, you can see that the first cell, the 3-event cell, takes the same triangular shape I said was fundamental to African-derived music in the previous chapter, and the second, 2-event cell, creates an oblique line. To derive musical meaning from those shapes, let's combine those two cells into one 5-event cell. But before we do, let's take a minute to observe what won't change when we perform that and other operations on rhythmic cells.

> 让我们更仔细地审视 3-2 节奏框架。观察这两个 cell，你可以看到第一个 cell，即 3-event cell，呈现出我在前一章中提到的非洲裔音乐基础的那种三角形形状，而第二个 2-event cell 则形成了一条斜线。为了从这些形状中推导出音乐含义，让我们将这两个 cell 组合成一个 5-event cell。但在我们这样做之前，让我们花点时间观察一下，当我们对节奏 cell 执行该操作以及其他操作时，哪些东西不会改变。

Imagine a group of five kindergarten kids walking into their classroom at Cell Academy: Kobi, Destiny, Ryu, Sam, and Alondra. If we change the order and put Sam and Alondra ahead of Kobi, Destiny, and Ryu, it's still the same set of kids. When they show up in pajamas on Pajama Day, then wear superhero costumes the next day because it's Halloween, they're still the same set of kids. If we give them a basketball and send them out on the court together in their street clothes, they're still the same set of kids. Their identity as a set of kindergarten students is invariant. The same applies to rhythmic cells. They remain the same cells when we change how we measure them or change a particular cell's location in time. With this in mind, we'll examine what gets created when the cells are joined.

> 想象五个幼儿园孩子走进 Cell Academy 的教室：Kobi、Destiny、Ryu、Sam 和 Alondra。如果我们改变顺序，把 Sam 和 Alondra 放在 Kobi、Destiny 和 Ryu 前面，仍然是同一组孩子。当他们在 Pajama Day 穿着睡衣出现，第二天因为是 Halloween 而穿上超级英雄服装时，他们仍然是同一组孩子。如果我们给他们一个篮球，让他们穿着便服一起上球场，他们仍然是同一组孩子。他们作为一组幼儿园学生的身份是不变的。同样的情况适用于有节奏的 cell。当我们改变描述它们的方式或改变某个特定 cell 在时间中的位置时，它们仍然是相同的 cell。带着这个想法，我们将探究当 cell 被连接在一起时会产生什么。

The first thing to note is that when we join the two cells, we produce a new cell that has twice the number of events of the original cell. In other words, this cell contains the number of events we get when we add the two cells together. Combining the two cells multiplies the number of events that the new cell will contain, and it doubles the cell's length as well. Using traditional Western musical terms, we could say that we're combining two bars of 2/4 time to create one bar of 4/4 time. Or if we measured it using cut time, we would say that the cell is two measures long.

> 首先需要注意的是，当我们连接两个 cell 时，会产生一个新的 cell，其事件数量是原始 cell 的两倍。换句话说，这个 cell 包含的是将两个 cell 相加后得到的事件数量。合并两个 cell 会使新 cell 包含的事件数量翻倍，同时其长度也会加倍。用传统西方音乐术语来说，我们可以说这是将两个 2/4 拍的小节合并成一个 4/4 拍的小节。或者，如果我们用二分拍来计量，我们会说这个 cell 的长度是两小节。

Periodicity is a fundamental property of music. When we say something has periodicity, we're saying that thing repeats in a predictable way. The vibratory activity that creates musical sound happens in the real world in real time, so we measure music's periodicity by reference to time. When we speak about the periodicity of music, we're speaking about cycles of activity that are happening at multiple scales. Musical events with a periodicity that we measure using repetitions per second as a reference are called tones or pitches.

> 周期性是音乐的一个基本属性。当我们说某物具有周期性时，我们指的是该事物以可预测的方式重复。产生音乐声音的振动活动在现实世界中实时发生，因此我们通过参照时间来测量音乐的周期性。当我们谈论音乐的周期性时，我们指的是在多个尺度上发生的活动循环。以每秒重复次数为参照来测量其周期性的音乐事件被称为音调或音高。

When we measure musical events using repetitions per minute as the reference, we say those events happen at a particular beat or tempo. The circle used in clock notation represents a cell's period, the time span of the cycle of rhythmic events represented by the geometric shape placed on it. African and African-derived music commonly operates at multiple periodicities. The 3-event cell embedded inside the 3-2 frame that moves at half that frame's speed is one example of that.

> 当我们以每分钟重复次数为参照来测量音乐事件时，我们说这些事件以特定的节拍或速度发生。时钟记谱法中使用的圆圈代表一个 cell 的周期，即放置于其上的几何形状所表示的节奏事件循环的时间跨度。非洲及其衍生音乐通常以多种周期性运作。嵌入在 3-2 框架内并以该框架一半速度移动的 3-event cell 就是其中一个例子。

Time is a musical cell's life force. The methods we use to measure time presuppose repetition. That means our relationship to time is, at a fundamental level, a relationship to rhythm. Black music is a creative practice that foregrounds forms of cyclic interactions humans recognize as rhythmic. When we engage with musical rhythm, we enter into a relationship with time. Our bodies process and parse rhythms, then frame time in relationship to them. We form one frame that encompasses the entirety of a perceived rhythm, as well as one or more frames that are our internal choice or choices we make to divide the frame so we can interact with the rhythm that made it. Pulse is one of the terms used to refer to the information generated by this process of parsing and demarcation.

> 时间是一个音乐 cell 的生命力。我们用来度量时间的方法预设了重复。这意味着，从根本层面上看，我们与时间的关系是一种与节奏的关系。黑人音乐是一种创造性实践，它凸显了人类视为节奏的循环互动形式。当我们参与音乐节奏时，便进入了一种与时间的关系。我们的身体处理和解析节奏，然后根据这些节奏来框架时间。我们形成一个涵盖了整个感知节奏的框架，同时形成一个或多个内部选择或我们用来划分该框架的选择，以便我们能够与产生该框架的节奏进行互动。脉动是用于指代这种解析和划分过程所生成的信息的术语之一。

A particular piece of music's pulse is framed by the method of analysis or means of measurement used to generate it. On one level, the pulse is wherever you feel it is. But on the level of the community of dancers, listeners, and the composer or composers of the piece of music you're listening to, there is an agreed-upon "correct" pulse, or multiple pulses. As I said earlier, salsa music, as well as other forms of African-derived music found in the Spanish Caribbean, is notated in cut time, which means the composers and musicians who create and perform the music see it as having a pulse with eight equally spaced events. Eddy Torres, the masterful salsa dancer who became a noted dance instructor, also taught his students that the music has an eight-beat pulse.[^1-4]

> 一段特定音乐的脉动，是由用于生成它的分析方法或测量手段所框架的。在一个层面上，脉动就在你感觉它所在之处。但在舞者、听众以及你所听乐曲作曲家的社群层面上，存在一种公认的“正确”脉动，或多个脉动。正如我之前所说，萨尔萨音乐以及西班牙加勒比地区其他源自非洲的音乐形式，都采用切割拍号记谱，这意味着创作和演奏这些音乐的作曲家和音乐家认为它有一个由八个等距事件构成的脉动。Eddy Torres，这位技艺精湛的萨尔萨舞者后来成为著名舞蹈教练，他也告诉他的学生，这种音乐有一个八拍脉动。[^1-4]

Up in the South Bronx, musicians showed the pulse by playing the clave. A conductor shows an orchestra the music's pulse by waving his baton. Bands "count off" their songs using evenly spaced shouts or drumstick clicks to set the music's pulse. People who make music using computers, phones, or other electronic gear use the device's metronome to set the pulse. On the other hand, Ornette Coleman, one of the most important musical figures of the twentieth century, who pioneered a form of music that came to be called free jazz, didn't count off his music to set its pulse. He would just say, "Ready? Play," and the band would start. The music's pulse would emerge out of the interaction between musicians.

> 在 South Bronx，音乐家们通过演奏 clave 来展示节拍。指挥家通过挥舞指挥棒向乐团展示音乐的节拍。乐队通过均匀间隔的喊叫或鼓棒敲击来“数拍”他们的歌曲，以设定音乐的节拍。使用计算机、手机或其他电子设备制作音乐的人使用设备的节拍器来设定节拍。另一方面，Ornette Coleman，二十世纪最重要的音乐人物之一，开创了一种后来被称为自由爵士的音乐形式，他并不通过数拍来设定音乐的节拍。他只会说：“准备好了吗？开始，”然后乐队就开始演奏。音乐的节拍从音乐家之间的互动中显现出来。

A piece of music that parses the same pulse in multiple ways can, and in practice often does, contain multiple periods. When we made the mental leap of combining the 3-event cell and the 2-event cell to create the 5-event cell, we experienced that for ourselves. We went from thinking about the rhythm as having two periods of a defined length that are measured in units of 8 to thinking about it as having one period of double that length that now has 16 of the units of measurement we'd defined to analyze the rhythm.

> 一段以多种方式解析相同脉冲的音乐可以（在实践中也常常）包含多个周期。当我们进行思维跳跃，将 3-event cell 和 2-event cell 组合成 5-event cell 时，我们亲身经历了这一点。我们从将节奏视为具有两个定义长度的周期（以 8 为单位测量）转变为将其视为一个长度为两倍的周期，现在有 16 个我们定义用于分析节奏的测量单位。

I've illustrated 2-, 3-, and 5-note rhythm cells using cells with 8 and 16 divisions. Rhythms that have cycles that divide into 8 or 16 even divisions are called duple or binary rhythms, because their cycles can be divided by two. In Africa and in African-derived music in the West, there are also many rhythms that have time periods with 12 divisions. Michael Jackson's song "The Way You Make Me Feel" and the classic rock staple "Dazed and Confused" by Led Zeppelin are two examples of songs based in African-derived traditions that are based on rhythms with 12 divisions. And in the United States, there are many rhythms that African Americans use that have cycles with 24 divisions. Kendrick Lamar's "heart pt. 6" is just one in a long history of songs with 24-division rhythms that includes "Look-Ka Py Py" by the Meters, "Mothership Connection" by Parliament, and "Warning" by Notorious B.I.G., among many, many others. Those rhythms are referred to as triple or ternary rhythms, because their cycles can be divided by three. In practice, there are many circumstances where binary and ternary rhythm cycles are deployed simultaneously. In the trap music era, it became common for rappers to use a ternary flow over music with binary rhythms. There is a 5-event rhythmic frame that is ubiquitous in West African music with ternary time periods. Musicologists call it the standard pattern.

> 我使用 8 和 16 个划分的节奏单元展示了 2 音符、3 音符和 5 音符节奏单元。那些周期分为 8 或 16 个等分的节奏被称为双拍或二进制节奏，因为它们的周期可以被 2 整除。在非洲以及西方受非洲影响的音乐中，还有许多节奏的时值周期包含 12 个划分。Michael Jackson 的歌曲 "The Way You Make Me Feel" 以及 Led Zeppelin 的经典摇滚曲目 "Dazed and Confused" 就是两个基于 12 划分节奏的非洲传统衍生歌曲的例子。而在美国，非裔美国人使用的许多节奏具有 24 个划分的周期。Kendrick Lamar 的 "heart pt. 6" 只是 24 划分节奏悠久历史中的一首，其他例子还包括 The Meters 的 "Look-Ka Py Py"、Parliament 的 "Mothership Connection" 以及 Notorious B.I.G. 的 "Warning" 等等。这些节奏被称为三拍或三进制节奏，因为它们的周期可以被 3 整除。在实践中，许多情况下二进制节奏和三进制节奏周期会同时使用。在陷阱音乐时代，说唱歌手在二进制节奏的音乐上使用三进制流动变得很常见。在西非音乐中，存在一种在 5 个事件基础上构成的节奏框架，且常与三进制时值结合使用。音乐学家将其称为标准模式。

This similarity is often exploited when a rhythmic frame's period is being divided in a binary and ternary fashion simultaneously in West African and Afro-Caribbean music. There are circumstances where the difference between binary and ternary rhythms is blurred and situations where both cyclic melding strategies, dual deployment, and blurring exist. For example, when jazz is written using Western musical notation, it is often written using a 16-event division even though the music is played using a 24-event division. Jazz musicians know when to utilize which period and have developed methods for exploiting the difference between them.

> 我们可以看到，其几何结构与图 6 所示的 5 事件节奏框架相似。这种相似性在西非和非洲裔加勒比音乐中经常被利用，当节奏框架的周期同时以二进制和三进制方式划分时。在某些情况下，二进制和三进制节奏之间的差异变得模糊，并且存在两种循环融合策略、双重部署以及模糊化的情况。例如，当爵士乐使用西方音乐记谱法书写时，通常采用 16 事件划分，尽管实际演奏中采用的是 24 事件划分。爵士音乐家知道何时使用哪种周期，并已发展出利用两者差异的方法。

One thing jazz and Latin music have in common is that neither can be captured properly using Western musical notation. The Latin musicians I've worked with have established something resembling cordial diplomatic relations with it. But quite a few jazz musicians I know have not and developed their own idiosyncratic forms of musical notation instead. Some, like Pulitzer Prize finalist Wadada Leo Smith, eschew Western notation entirely and create graphic scores that are both works of art and encapsulations of musical compositions. Clock notation works well for illustrating many of the aspects of Black music that are hard to notice, or get hidden, using Western notation. So that's what I'm using in this book the majority of the time. But let's be clear. The map is not the territory. The territory is the music, and especially music's relationship to the body and mind, and the effect those have on the world.

> 爵士乐和拉丁音乐有一个共同点：两者都无法用西方音乐记谱法准确记录。与我合作过的拉丁音乐家已经与这种记谱法建立了一种类似友好外交关系的东西。但我认识的不少爵士音乐家却没有这样做，而是发展出了自己独特的音乐记谱形式。有些人，比如普利策奖决赛入围者 Wadada Leo Smith，完全摒弃了西方记谱法，创作出既是艺术品又蕴含音乐作品的图形乐谱。钟表记谱法能很好地展现黑人音乐中那些用西方记谱法难以察觉或被隐藏的许多方面。因此，我在本书中大部分时间都使用这种方法。但需要明确的是，地图并非疆域本身。疆域是音乐，尤其是音乐与身心的关系，以及这些对世界产生的影响。

***

My lessons with Papo showed me that musical styles have parents and lineages, and gave me insight into how the environment that surrounds those lineages affects them. In his apartment, I heard the rhythms that traveled to Cuba from the Congo and heard the son montuno that developed as a result of them being on that island for a while. I could hear how the Puerto Ricans living in New York City then morphed those rhythms further after they got hold of them on the streets in The Bronx. Every time I traveled up there, I saw clear examples of how we affect our environment and how our environment affects us.

> 我与 Papo 的学习让我明白音乐风格有其父母和谱系，并让我深入了解围绕这些谱系的环境如何影响它们。在他的公寓里，我听到了从刚果传到古巴的节奏，也听到了因这些节奏在岛上停留一段时间而发展出的 son montuno。我能听出生活在纽约市的波多黎各人如何在布朗克斯区的街头掌握这些节奏后，进一步将其变形。每次我去那里，都清楚地看到我们如何影响环境，以及环境如何影响我们的例子。

One of my aunt Rachel's favorite foods was pig's feet. She'd usually send my sister to go get them at the bodega near where she lived, but once in a while she'd send me. In that bodega, I'd see and smell Southern fried chicken's Latin cousins, pollo frito and chicharrónes de pollo, and notice the similarities of, and differences between, the way Black Americans and Puerto Ricans cooked and consumed. Sometimes I'd buy fried plantains instead of potato chips. While the person behind the counter was opening the large jar of pig's feet that sat on the bodega's counter near the cash register, I watched and listened to the interactions happening inside the store. I'd listen out for Spanish phrases I could repeat, inevitably picking up the ones that kids weren't supposed to repeat. As the person behind the counter pulled the pig's feet out, wrapped them up, and then handed them to me, I began to gain an understanding of how communities collide, exchange, then combine to create new cultural forms. In the fertile, but less than neutral, ground where the Southern United States met Puerto Rico, I had my first taste of the sort of boundary breaching that would, in a few years, define my day-to-day movement.

> 我的一位姑姑 Rachel 最喜欢的食物之一是猪蹄。她通常会让我的姐姐去她家附近的杂货店买，但偶尔也会让我去。在那家杂货店里，我会看到并闻到南方炸鸡的拉丁亲戚——pollo frito 和 chicharrónes de pollo，并注意到美国黑人与波多黎各人在烹饪和饮食方式上的相似与差异。有时我会买炸大蕉片而不是薯片。当柜台后面的人打开放在收银台旁杂货柜台上的大罐猪蹄时，我观察并倾听着店里发生的互动。我会留心那些我能复述的西班牙语短语，不可避免地学会了一些小孩子不该重复的话。当柜台后面的人把猪蹄拿出来，包好，然后递给我时，我开始理解社区如何碰撞、交流，然后融合，创造出新的文化形式。在美国南部与波多黎各交汇的沃土上——尽管并非中立地带——我第一次尝到了那种跨越边界的滋味，而几年后，这种跨越将成为我日常生活的常态。


# Ⅱ FREQUENCIES AND DECIBELS
**频率与分贝**

My father sold the wooden multifamily house he owned in Bedford-Stuyvesant and moved the family into an apartment on the street that separates two other neighborhoods in Brooklyn, Flatbush and Ditmas Park, in May 1967. At that time Bed-Stuy, as we call it, was the area with the largest Black population in America. Shortly after we moved, I discovered my new neighborhood was different. There was a grand total of two other Black families on our block. A couple of years later I did an estimate of how many people lived on our block for homework, taking the building I lived in as the basis for the neighborhood's population density. I estimated thirty-five thousand people lived on our block, and my teacher didn't dispute or attempt to correct my number. That number meant that with my family's move, Black people became 0.05 percent of my block's population.

> 我父亲卖掉了他在 Bedford-Stuyvesant 拥有的木质多户住宅，并于 1967 年 5 月将全家搬到了布鲁克林另外两个社区 Flatbush 和 Ditmas Park 交界处的一条街上的公寓里。当时，我们称之为 Bed-Stuy 的地方是美国黑人人口最多的地区。搬家后不久，我发现新社区有所不同。我们街区总共只有另外两户黑人家庭。几年后，我为了做作业估算了一下我们街区住了多少人，以我所住的大楼作为社区人口密度的基础。我估算我们街区住了三万五千人，而我的老师没有质疑或试图纠正我的数字。这个数字意味着，随着我家搬家，黑人人口占我们街区总人口的 0.05%。

Those were the days of de facto segregation in New York City. Back then, when my father would drive downtown through Manhattan on the way home after visiting relatives in Harlem or The Bronx, I would always joke when we crossed Ninety-Sixth Street. I could plainly see what was supposed to be an invisible wall that prevented any Black life from establishing a foothold on the south side of that street. There, my father's car would become a spaceship filled with a family of intrepid explorers penetrating the force field surrounding the Upper East Side of Manhattan on their voyage home. Versions of that wall existed all over New York City. Some still persist today.

> 那是纽约市事实上的种族隔离时期。那时候，我父亲去哈莱姆或布朗克斯探亲后，开车穿过曼哈顿市中心回家时，每次我们穿过九十六街，我都会开玩笑。我能清楚地看到一道本应无形的墙，阻止任何黑人在那条街的南侧立足。在那里，我父亲的车会变成一艘宇宙飞船，载着我们这个由无畏探险者组成的家庭，在回家的航程中突破环绕曼哈顿上东区的力场。类似的墙在纽约市到处都有。有些至今依然存在。

The border between Flatbush and Ditmas Park ran down the middle of the street I lived on. I lived on the Ditmas Park side of the street, so that's where I went to school. I was in third grade when I started. They put me in class 3-8. In those days they rated the classes in numeric order by academic achievement. IGC—intellectually gifted children—was the top of the heap. Next was 3-1, 3-2, and so on down to 3-10 or 3-11 depending on how dense the neighborhood was. In Bed-Stuy I was always in the IGC classes. I loved to read. My father had taught me how to read when I was four. He loved to tell the story of how my kindergarten teacher ran up to him after the first day of school and asked him, "Mr. Gibbs, did you know your son could read?" and he responded, "Yes I did. I taught him." So I was a bit shocked, and more than a bit upset when they put me in 3-8. They might have put me in that class because that's where the kids who were bused in from Brownsville, a Black neighborhood near Bed-Stuy that was then and still is one of the highest-crime neighborhoods in New York City, were. Did they think I'd be more comfortable there? I definitely was not. The class was reading books I'd read back in first grade. There wasn't a lot of learning going on there.

> Flatbush 和 Ditmas Park 之间的边界沿着我所住街道的中间延伸。我住在街道的 Ditmas Park 一侧，所以我在那里上学。我入学时是三年级。他们把我安排在 3-8 班。那个时候，他们按照学业成绩对学生进行排名，班级顺序按数字排列。IGC——智力超群儿童班——是最高等级。接下来是 3-1 班、3-2 班，依次往下到 3-10 或 3-11 班，具体取决于社区的密度。在 Bed-Stuy 时，我一直都在 IGC 班级。我热爱阅读。我父亲在我四岁时就教会了我阅读。他喜欢讲这个故事：我上幼儿园的第一天过后，我的老师跑上前问他：“Gibbs 先生，您知道您的儿子会读书吗？”他回答说：“是的，我知道。是我教他的。”所以当他们把我安排在 3-8 班时，我有点震惊，而且远不止是有点沮丧。他们把我安排在那个班，可能是因为那个班里都是从布朗斯维尔（一个靠近 Bed-Stuy 的黑人社区，当时和现在都是纽约市犯罪率最高的社区之一）校车接来的孩子。他们觉得我在那里会更自在吗？我绝对不自在。那个班在读的书我一年级就读过了。那里没有学到多少东西。

So I hatched a plan to get out. I'd noticed that in the back of the class there was a collection of reading comprehension books that contained essays and included a multiple-choice test after each. They were color-coded by grades that went from grade one to six. During a break in class, I grabbed the whole stack of them, and to be sure my point was made, read every essay and filled in every test in every book. At the end of the break, I handed the entire stack to the teacher, being sure to place the most advanced book on top. I remember that she put the entire stack on her desk. I don't remember exactly what she did with the books immediately after she put them on her desk, but I do fondly remember that a few seconds later, she left the room holding the purple book, which was the most advanced, with a shocked look on her face. She came back a few minutes later and told me to grab my stuff and go to the principal's office. The principal gave me something like an apology and sent me to the IGC class.

> 于是我制定了一个出逃计划。我注意到教室后面有一堆阅读理解书，里面包含文章，每篇文章后还有选择题。这些书按年级用颜色编码，从一年级到六年级。课间休息时，我抓起整摞书，为了确保我的意图被理解，我读完了每一篇文章，填完了每一本书里的所有测试题。休息结束时，我把整摞书交给老师，特意把最难的放在最上面。我记得她把整摞书放在桌上。我不太记得她放好后立即做了什么，但我清楚地记得，几秒钟后，她拿着那本最难的紫色书离开了教室，脸上带着震惊的表情。几分钟后她回来了，让我收拾东西去校长办公室。校长给了我类似于道歉的话，然后把我送到了 IGC 班。

Intellectually, that class was what I was used to. But culturally, it was a whole new world. I was the only Black boy in the class. It's odd for me to think about it now, but I didn't manage to bond with the class's equally solitary Black girl. A large majority of the class was Jewish, with a couple of Catholics thrown in for spice. If there was another Protestant in the class they kept that info to themselves. The kids in my class mostly had white-collar parents: doctors, lawyers, management types. The kids on my block, on the other hand, mostly had blue-collar parents. A couple of them had fireman fathers. A kid who lived on the next block could teach me what a Zamboni was because his father was a teamster who drove the one at Madison Square Garden. They tended to be Catholic, and quite a few of them went to Catholic schools instead of public school.

> 从智力上讲，那个班是我习惯的。但从文化上讲，那是一个全新的世界。我是班上唯一的黑人男孩。现在回想起来，这很奇怪，但我没能和班上同样孤独的那个黑人女孩建立起联系。班上绝大多数是犹太人，还有几个天主教徒作为点缀。如果有其他新教徒在班上，他们也对此闭口不谈。我班上的孩子大多父母是白领：医生、律师、管理阶层。而我街区的孩子们，大多数父母是蓝领。有几个人的父亲是消防员。隔壁街区的一个孩子能告诉我什么是 Zamboni（赞博尼磨冰机），因为他父亲是卡车司机，在麦迪逊广场花园开那台机器。他们大多是天主教徒，不少人上的是天主教学校而不是公立学校。

The kids in my class, for the most part, accepted me. I didn't really sweat the change in habitat. Existing in white spaces wasn't foreign to me because, in his own way, my father was a trailblazer. Around the time I was born, he was the one who integrated the previously all-white Kings Highway United Methodist Church, which was two blocks from where he worked as a janitor in the Flatlands section of Brooklyn, which back then was also all-white. But that was only a matter of spending an hour and a half in Sunday school, an hour and a half in church, then hopping in the car back to Bed-Stuy. It wasn't a 24/7 thing.

> 班上的孩子们大部分接受了我。我并不太在意环境的改变。在白人空间里生活对我来说并不陌生，因为我的父亲在他自己的方式下是个开拓者。我出生前后，正是他打破了金斯公路联合卫理公会的白人壁垒，这个教堂离他工作的布鲁克林 Flatlands 区的清洁工岗位有两个街区远，那时那个区也是全白人社区。但那不过是在主日学校待一个半小时，在教堂待一个半小时，然后跳上车回 Bed-Stuy 。那不是全天候的事情。

Once my family moved to Ditmas Park, I lived a dual existence. During the week, I spent my time usually being the Black person in otherwise all-white spaces. On the weekends, Dad would load the family into his car, and we'd go be just people. We'd go visit my parents' best friends, the Holidays, who lived in the projects in Fort Greene in Brooklyn. Or we'd visit my grandmother, my Dad's mom. The list of the neighborhoods she lived in is a rundown of the era's worst New York City neighborhoods. When I was a preschooler, she lived in Far Rockaway, on the tip of that island at the far end of the borough of Queens. When we moved from Bed-Stuy she was living in the East New York section of Brooklyn, which borders on Brownsville and has a similar crime rate. By the time I started junior high school, she had moved to the South Bronx, then and now the poorest congressional district in the US.

> 一旦我家搬到 Ditmas Park，我就过着双重生活。在工作日，我通常是在原本全部由白人组成的空间中唯一的黑人。在周末，爸爸会把全家人塞进他的车里，我们出去就只是普通人。我们会去拜访我父母最好的朋友，Holidays 一家，他们住在布鲁克林 Fort Greene 的政府公房里。或者我们会去探望我的祖母，也就是我父亲的妈妈。她住过的街区名单，就是那个时代纽约市最差街区的缩略图。当我还在上幼儿园时，她住在 Far Rockaway，位于皇后区最远端那个岛屿的尖端。当我们从 Bed-Stuy 搬走时，她住在布鲁克林的 East New York 区，该区与 Brownsville 接壤，犯罪率也差不多。等我开始上初中的时候，她已经搬到了 South Bronx，那里在当时和现在都是美国最穷的国会选区。

After a few years, she moved to the Soundview section of The Bronx. That's the neighborhood where the most feared NYC street gang of the pre-hip-hop era, the Black Spades, was born, and that's also the area where the Zulu Nation, the group that grew out of the NYC gang truce that codified the Five Elements of hip-hop, came together. Aunt Rachel, my mother's sister, also lived in the South Bronx. She lived in the area that became known nationwide as Fort Apache. When I was a teenager, there was a period of time when I could ride the subway home from her apartment for free because the neighborhood train station had been firebombed. On other weekends, we would visit my Dad's aunt Liz, or his sister, my aunt Marie, in Harlem. Aunt Liz, my great-aunt, was the matriarch of my father's family. Her apartment in the Manhattanville projects was the spot where the relatives on my side of the family who lived in the New York area would get together.

> 几年后，她搬到了布朗克斯区的 Soundview 区。那个街区是嘻哈时代前纽约市最令人恐惧的街头帮派 Black Spades 的诞生地，也是 Zulu Nation 的聚集地，该团体源于纽约市帮派休战，并由此确立了嘻哈五元素。我母亲的姐姐 Rachel 姨妈也住在南布朗克斯。她住在一个全国闻名、被称为 Fort Apache 的地区。当我十几岁时，有一段时间我可以从她的公寓免费乘地铁回家，因为附近的火车站被燃烧弹袭击过。在其他周末，我们会去哈莱姆区看望我父亲的姨妈 Liz，或者他的妹妹，我的 Marie 姨妈。Liz 姨妈，我的姑姥姥，是我父亲家族的女家长。她在 Manhattanville 公共住房项目的公寓是我这边住在纽约地区的亲戚们聚会的地方。

For me, the places my family would travel to on the weekends were safe places. On the other hand, the spaces that the parents of the kids in my class saw as safe spaces, "nice" suburban towns with cul-de-sacs, were the most dangerous and scary places I could go. Equally dangerous were those "nice" New York City neighborhoods where people of the Catholic faith were the main population. Places like Bensonhurst in Brooklyn and Howard Beach in Queens.

> 对我来说，周末家人会去的那些地方是安全的。而另一方面，我班同学父母视为安全空间的地方 —— 那些带有死胡同的“好”郊区小镇 —— 则是我能去的最危险、最可怕的地方。同样危险的还有那些“好”的纽约市街区，那里天主教徒占主要人口，比如布鲁克林的 Bensonhurst 和皇后区的 Howard Beach。

One of the only safe spaces in those neighborhoods, and a refuge in my own, were hobby shops. Every time I first walked into one, I had to surprise them the same way I did in my 3-8 class at PS 139. The shock I caused by entering would always dissipate when I went to the section of the store where the chemistry sets were and started talking about mine.

> 在这些街区中，唯一安全的地方之一，以及在我自己社区里的避难所，就是爱好商店。每次我初次走进一家这样的店，都必须像我在 PS 139 学校的 3 - 8 年级班级里那样，给他们带来同样的惊讶。我进门引起的震惊，总会在我走到店里摆放化学实验套装的区域、开始谈论我的那一套的时候消散。

Chemistry sets were mini–home laboratories marketed to children. They were made up of an array of gear that included a box that held bottles of various chemical substances; a selection of glassware you'd find in a lab; test tubes, droppers, flasks, beakers, and so on; and other lab equipment, including pH strips, Bunsen burners, and microscopes. I started building one in fourth grade, when I won my school's science fair. A few supportive adults in the neighborhood, seemingly without coordination, had individually given me components for what ended up being a pretty substantial set. One gave a microscope. Another contributed some flasks, a Bunsen burner, and a few bottles of chemicals. Still another gave me a box of chemicals, some test tubes, and a flask. Others supported my scientific exploration in their own way. When a couple who lived in the building next to mine spotted me reading geology books in the local library, they revealed that they were rock collectors. With my parents' permission, they took me to Inwood Hill Park in Manhattan. There, they showed me the different types of rocks and explained how the various geological formations in the park had occurred. Another couple showed me how to use wax paper and an ironing board to preserve leaves, and gave me a scrapbook to put them in. I borrowed a photo book from the library that identified tree leaves in the area, then put together a scrapbook containing specimens from twenty-five different species. The supportive actions of those adults helped build my intellectual foundation and contributed to my next two science fair wins.

> 化学实验套装是面向儿童销售的迷你家庭实验室。它们由一系列器材组成，包括一个装有各种化学物质瓶子的盒子；一套实验室常见的玻璃器皿，如试管、滴管、烧瓶、烧杯等；以及其他实验设备，包括 pH 试纸、本生灯和显微镜。我在四年级时开始搭建一套这样的装置，当时我赢得了学校的科学展。社区里几位热心的成年人，似乎没有事先商量，各自给了我一些组件，最终凑成了一套相当完整的设备。一位给了我显微镜。另一位贡献了一些烧瓶、一盏本生灯和几瓶化学品。还有一位给了我一盒化学品、一些试管和一个烧瓶。其他人则以自己的方式支持我的科学探索。当住在隔壁楼的一对夫妇发现我在当地图书馆阅读地质学书籍时，他们透露自己是岩石收藏家。在我父母允许下，他们带我去了曼哈顿的因伍德山公园。在那里，他们向我展示了不同类型的岩石，并解释了公园中各种地质构造的形成原因。另一对夫妇教我如何用蜡纸和熨衣板保存树叶，并给了我一本剪贴簿来存放它们。我从图书馆借了一本摄影集，用来识别当地的树叶，然后整理了一本包含 25 种不同树种标本的剪贴簿。这些成年人的支持行为帮助奠定了我的知识基础，并为我接下来两次科学展获奖做出了贡献。

In the sixth grade, I joined my church's basketball team. We would play against other Protestant church teams in Brooklyn. Our opponents were members of Lutheran, Methodist, Baptist, Dutch Reformed, and Episcopal congregations. The Lutheran, Episcopal, and Dutch Reformed basketball teams, like their churches, tended to be all white. The Baptist teams tended to be all Black. The Methodist teams were in various stages of integration. That first year, the Lutheran and Baptist teams both gave me grief because I was the only Black player on an otherwise all-white team. On the court, I didn't care. Off the court was a different story.

> 在六年级时，我加入了教会的篮球队。我们将与布鲁克林的其他新教教会球队比赛。我们的对手来自路德宗、卫理公会、浸信会、荷兰归正会和圣公会的会众。路德宗、圣公会和荷兰归正会的篮球队，就像他们的教会一样，往往全是白人。浸信会的球队则往往全是黑人。卫理公会的球队处于不同阶段的融合中。那第一年，路德宗和浸信会的球队都让我感到困扰，因为我是唯一一个黑人球员，而其他队友全是白人。在球场上，我并不在意。但在场下，情况就不同了。

The Baptist teams tended to leave the trash-talking on the court. And they usually won, sometimes beating us as badly as the era's showiest basketball team, the Harlem Globetrotters, beat their hapless opponents, the Washington Generals. That meant they'd be in a good mood after the game. When we played the Lutheran team, the roles switched—we got to be the Globetrotters while they played the stooge. That wasn't so good for me. When the Lutherans played the Baptist churches, they couldn't talk a lot of trash after the game. When our games with them were over, they had a solitary target to aim their anger at. I often felt the way a lone impala must feel crossing the Maasai Mara when I walked to the bus stop after playing a Lutheran team. I was constantly on the lookout for packs of boys. Whether it was a basketball player I'd inadvertently embarrassed by being good and Black who was hanging out with his crew or a group of boys seeking to raise themselves up in the neighborhood's hierarchy who spotted me—getting caught alone while Black by a group of white boys was going to be a problem.

> 浸礼会球队往往把垃圾话留在球场上。而且他们通常能赢，有时甚至像那个时代最炫酷的篮球队——哈林环球旅行者队——痛击他们不幸的对手华盛顿将军队一样，把我们打得落花流水。这意味着比赛结束后他们会心情不错。当我们与路德宗球队比赛时，角色就互换了——我们成了环球旅行者队，而他们则扮演陪衬的角色。这对我不太妙。当路德宗球队与浸礼会球队比赛时，他们赛后不能说太多垃圾话。但当我们与他们的比赛结束后，他们就有了一个可以集中发泄愤怒的孤独目标。每次打完路德宗球队后，我步行去公交车站时，常常感觉自己就像一只独自穿越马赛马拉的瞪羚。我时刻警惕着成群的男孩。不管是一个被我无意中羞辱的篮球队员——只因为我又优秀又是黑人，而他正和同伴们闲逛——还是一群试图在社区等级中提升自己地位的男孩发现了我——作为一个黑人，独自被一群白人男孩抓到，那肯定是个麻烦。

By the end of that first season, I'd learned that being the only Black kid on the team wasn't going to work, either for my ego or my safety. So I convinced a couple of the other Black kids who had moved into the neighborhood to join the team. I wasn't the best player on the team anymore, but that was good. We no longer had to be the Washington Generals. And we got to be the Globetrotters a lot more often.

> 到第一个赛季结束时，我明白了作为球队里唯一的黑人孩子是行不通的，无论是对我的自尊还是我的安全。所以我说服了几个搬进这个社区的其他黑人孩子加入球队。我不再是球队里最好的球员了，但这样很好。我们不再需要扮演 Washington Generals 了。而且我们更经常能成为 Globetrotters。

Over time, the complexion of the neighborhood began to change, and by the time I graduated junior high school five years later, the vast majority of the third graders starting class in the public school I'd gone to were Black. Somewhere along the line, the slow influx of Black people into the neighborhood hit an invisible tipping point, and the blue-collar adults who lived in the apartments in the neighborhood exited en masse. The professionals who lived in large houses on the tree-lined streets near the public school took a lot longer to leave. But the majority of them eventually sold their houses and moved as well.

> 随着时间的推移，这个社区的面貌开始改变，到五年后我初中毕业时，我所就读的公立学校开始上课的三年级学生绝大多数都是黑人。在某个时候，黑人缓慢涌入社区达到了一个无形的临界点，住在社区公寓里的蓝领成年人集体离开了。那些住在公立学校附近绿树成荫的街道上的大房子里的专业人士花了更长的时间才离开。但他们中的大多数人最终还是卖掉了房子搬走了。

After I left elementary school, things changed for me also. The boundary for Ditmas Junior High School was one block closer in than the boundary for the elementary school. That meant the kids who lived on my street would go to junior high school across the border, in Flatbush, instead of in Ditmas Park. Like Ditmas Park, the worse-off people tended to live in apartments and the better off lived in houses. Flatbush had some of the large wooden houses that were typical in Ditmas Park. But the majority of the houses were three- or four-story buildings with a narrower footprint made of bricks, often covered with smooth sandstone, called brownstones.

> 离开小学后，我的生活也发生了变化。迪特马斯初中的学区边界比小学的学区边界向内缩了一个街区。这意味着住在我那条街上的孩子要去边界外的弗拉特布什上初中，而不是在迪特马斯公园。和迪特马斯公园一样，经济条件较差的人往往住在公寓里，而条件较好的人则住在独栋房屋中。弗拉特布什有一些迪特马斯公园典型的大型木屋。但大多数房屋是三四层楼的砖结构建筑，占地面积较窄，通常覆盖着光滑的砂岩，被称为褐石建筑。

Another big difference was that the section of Flatbush surrounding my junior high school had a sizable contingent of Black people who lived there. There were Black people whose parents came from the American South living in Flatbush, including friends of mine from church. But the majority of the Black people who lived in Flatbush traced their ancestry back to an island in the Caribbean.

> 另一个重大区别是，我初中所在的弗拉特布什区域居住着相当数量的黑人。那里有父母来自美国南方的黑人，包括我在教堂认识的一些朋友。但住在弗拉特布什的大多数黑人的祖先可以追溯到加勒比海的某个岛屿。

A big personal change came in junior high school, when I was offered the chance to join the Special Progress program and complete the three years of junior high school in two years. I'd been offered the option of skipping ahead a year every year from the third year on. The offer was always followed by advice to stay in the grade I was in. In junior high school, I decided to disregard that advice. Everything was cool until the school science fair. I came in second in my grade at school and only got an honorable mention at Brooklyn's borough-wide junior high school science fair. That might sound good to you. But for me, it was like Italian auto racer and entrepreneur Enzo Ferrari said: Second is the first of the losers. I didn't know how to take it.

> 一个重大的个人改变发生在初中，当时我有机会加入 Special Progress 计划，并在两年内完成三年的初中课程。从三年级起，我每年都得到跳级一年的选择。这个提议总是伴随着留在当前年级的建议。在初中，我决定无视那个建议。一切都很顺利，直到学校的科学展览会。我在年级里得了第二名，在布鲁克林区级的初中科学展览会上只获得了荣誉奖。那对你来说可能听起来不错。但对我来说，就像意大利赛车手兼企业家 Enzo Ferrari 所说：第二名是失败者中的第一名。我不知道如何接受。

At that young age, I'd gleaned some of the defining characteristics of the multiple cultures I learned to navigate. I didn't have the words for it then, but my life was giving me a practical understanding of how demographics shape culture. And, because my education and cultural knowledge didn't map cleanly to my demographic slot, my father's trailblazing had made me a trailblazer too. I was comfortable in my skin, but well aware that many of the people in the territories I passed through saw me as unique, and found my novelty perplexing. Lack of mentorship, and especially lack of mentorship from successful people who understood my struggle and its pitfalls, was my issue then, and it's still a problem for Black youth today. If someone had sat me down and said these things happen, walked me through why, and showed me how to do better next time, things would have changed. If someone had guided me toward doing science in a way that was directly related to who I am—for example, told me that I could create a survey that gave me a nonthreatening reason to ask the old ladies who crossed the street when they saw me why they did so, which I could then collate into a study—I would have stuck it out. I didn't quit my education; it was still important to me. But that loss signified a turning point.

> 在那个年纪，我已经学会了在多种文化中游刃有余的一些特征。当时我无法用语言描述，但生活让我切实理解了人口结构如何塑造文化。而且，由于我的教育背景和文化知识并不完全符合我的人口统计类别，父亲的开拓精神也让我成为了开拓者。我对自己感到自在，但也清楚意识到，在我所经过的领域中，许多人视我为异类，觉得我的新奇令人困惑。缺乏指导，尤其是缺乏那些理解我的挣扎及其陷阱的成功人士的指导，是我当时的问题，至今仍是黑人青年面临的问题。如果有人能坐下来告诉我这些事情会发生，向我解释原因，并教我下次如何做得更好，情况就会改变。如果有人能引导我以与自身身份直接相关的方式从事科学工作——例如，告诉我可以设计一份调查，以此作为非威胁性的理由，去询问那些看到我就过马路的老妇人为何如此，然后将其整理成研究——我本会坚持下去。我没有放弃学业；它对我来说仍然重要。但那次损失标志着一个转折点。

Right around that time, the local hobby shops stopped selling glue to children under eighteen because teens in the city were using it to get high. Building model cars and planes, which was a popular teen hobby pre-internet, was taken off of our tables. They also stopped selling chemistry sets. They were fun, but the fun they provided always carried a degree of danger. To scratch my science itch, I started going to the local electronics store. I bought a beginner's book there and started making little circuits at home. I was working my way up to making a little radio when Dad asked me to go to the audiologist with him. And that's where I was introduced to the concepts of frequencies and decibels.

> 就在那时，当地的模型店停止向 18 岁以下青少年出售胶水，因为城里的青少年用它来吸食。组装模型车和飞机，曾是互联网时代前青少年流行的爱好，也被从我们的桌面上取缔了。他们也不再出售化学实验套装。这些套装很有趣，但它们带来的乐趣总是伴随着一定的危险。为了满足我对科学的渴望，我开始去当地的电子商店。我在那里买了一本入门书，并开始在家搭建小电路。我正逐步努力制作一个小收音机，这时爸爸让我和他一起去看听力专家。就在那里，我接触到了频率和分贝的概念。

***

Frequency is a term of measurement that refers to how often, how frequently, an occurrence happens during a specified period of time. In the case of my father, the audiologist was measuring the frequency of the sound pressure waves entering my father's left ear. The unit of measurement used is wave cycles per second, named hertz in recognition of Heinrich Hertz, the person who proved the existence of electromagnetic waves. One cycle per second is one hertz, written 1 Hz, ten thousand cycles per second is written 10,000 Hz or 10 kHz (the k is for kilo, which means "thousand" in Greek), and one million cycles per second is written 1,000 kHz or 1 MHz (M stands for mega, which means "extremely large" in Greek). The standard range of human hearing is 20 Hz to 20 kHz, with babies hearing slightly higher than 20 kHz and mature adults hearing less, topping out between 15 and 17 kHz.

> 频率是一个度量术语，指在特定时间段内事件发生的频繁程度。以我父亲为例，听力学家测量的是进入他左耳的声压波的频率。使用的测量单位是每秒波周期数，命名为赫兹（Hz），以纪念证明电磁波存在的海因里希·赫兹（Heinrich Hertz）。每秒一个周期即一赫兹，写作 1 Hz；每秒一万个周期写作 10,000 Hz 或 10 kHz（k 代表 kilo，在希腊语中意为“千”）；每秒一百万个周期写作 1,000 kHz 或 1 MHz（M 代表 mega，在希腊语中意为“极大”）。人类听觉的标准范围是 20 Hz 到 20 kHz，婴儿能听到略高于 20 kHz 的声音，而成年人的听力范围会缩小，最高在 15 到 17 kHz 之间。

Decibels are a measure of a sound's intensity. Decibels are measured on a logarithmic scale, which means that a change of 10 decibels is an order of magnitude of change—that is, 10 decibels' increase means the power of the sound has increased tenfold. The sound pressure level of the quietest sound someone with standard hearing can hear is zero decibels, written 0 dB. Ten decibels is ten times louder than 0 dB. Twenty decibels is ten times louder than 10 dB, and one hundred times louder than 0 dB. Thirty decibels is ten times louder than 20 dB, and one thousand times louder than 0 dB. The logarithmic Bel[^i] scale (named for Alexander Graham Bell, the person credited with inventing the telephone) is necessary because of the wide range of volume standard human hearing can absorb.

> 分贝是衡量声音强度的单位。分贝采用对数尺度进行测量，这意味着 10 分贝的变化对应一个数量级的变化——即增加 10 分贝表示声音的功率增加了十倍。具有标准听力的人能听到的最微弱声音的声压级为零分贝，写作 0 dB。10 分贝比 0 dB 大十倍。20 分贝比 10 dB 大十倍，比 0 dB 大一百倍。30 分贝比 20 dB 大十倍，比 0 dB 大一千倍。对数尺度的 Bel 标度（以亚历山大·格雷厄姆·贝尔命名，他被公认为电话的发明者）之所以必要，是因为标准人类听力所能吸收的音量范围非常广泛。

My father, who was hard of hearing, needed to go to the audiologist to get his hearing aid optimized. He wore one designed to facilitate hearing the human voice. It operated in a range slightly wider than what people in telecommunications call the voice frequency range, which is 300 to 3,400 Hz. A hearing aid like his amplifies frequencies from 200 to 5000 Hz. At the time we went to the audiologist, my father could still hear words if they were spoken directly in his ear. That meant his hearing aid had to be capable of increasing those sounds' intensity to a level where someone with moderate hearing loss could recognize them, in other words at least 40 dB.

> 我的父亲听力不佳，需要去看听力学家优化他的助听器。他佩戴了一款旨在帮助聆听人声的助听器。它的工作范围略宽于电信行业所称的语音频率范围，即 300 到 3,400 Hz。像他这样的助听器放大频率范围为 200 到 5000 Hz。在我们去看听力学家时，如果有人直接在他耳边说话，我父亲仍能听见。这意味着他的助听器必须能够将这些声音的强度提升到一定水平，使中度听力损失的人能够识别，也就是说至少 40 dB。

Hearing loss is a decrease in the dynamic range of sounds a person can hear. Moderate hearing loss means that the quietest sound you can hear is 41 dB louder (remember that's a number with 41 zeros) than a person with standard hearing. Severe hearing loss is 71 dB. At that level you wouldn't hear anything that's quieter than the volume of your vacuum cleaner. An unfettered rock band will play at 120 dB, a trillion times louder than the quietest audible sound, which is pushing the limit of safety. Louder than that, things get dicey. Jet planes and artillery fire measure 130 dB. One hundred and forty decibels of sound can cause pain, and 150 dB can cause permanent damage.

> 听力损失是指一个人能听到的声音动态范围减小。中度听力损失意味着你能听到的最安静声音，比标准听力的人高出 41 dB（记住这个数字后面有 41 个零）。重度听力损失为 71 dB。在这个水平下，你听不到任何比吸尘器音量更安静的声音。不受限制的摇滚乐队演奏时可达 120 dB，比可听见的最安静声音响亮一万亿倍，这已接近安全极限。再响亮一些，情况就会变得危险。喷气式飞机和火炮的响声可达 130 dB。140 dB 的声音会引起疼痛，而 150 dB 则会造成永久性损伤。

The audiologist used what are called pure tones to determine my father's ability to hear sounds. Most sounds we hear are complex, meaning they contain a spectrum of sound waves that vibrate at different frequencies. When audiologists examine someone's hearing, though, they use electronic equipment to synthesize sine waves, which are signals that have only a single frequency in their spectrum. These one-frequency sounds are called pure tones because their signal is clearly defined, and they are called simple tones in contradistinction to complex tones. The pure simplicity of sine waves facilitates precise measurement by the audiologist. They graph these measurements in a chart called a pure-tone audiogram.

> 听力学家使用所谓的纯音来测定我父亲的听力能力。我们听到的大部分声音都是复杂的，即它们包含一系列以不同频率振动的声波。然而，当听力学家检查一个人的听力时，他们会使用电子设备合成正弦波，这些信号的频谱中只有单一频率。这种单一频率的声音被称为纯音，因为它们的信号清晰明确，与复杂音相对，被称为简单音。正弦波的纯粹简单性有助于听力学家进行精确测量。他们将这些测量结果绘制在一张称为纯音听力图的表格中。

After that trip to the audiologist's office, I started to focus more on sound than on circuits. I'd shop at a store near my junior high school called Lafayette Radio Electronics. They didn't just sell electronic components and tools. They carried a full range of audio equipment. They had every kind of radio receiver and transmitter—including ham, CB, shortwave, police scanners, and VHF—in stock. They had a listening room set up where you could hear different combinations of home stereo, and later quad (4-channel) speaker and amplifier setups. They had another area where they demonstrated public address systems, combinations of speakers and amplifiers designed to disperse sound at locations where groups of people gather, and microphones. Another area had recording equipment—reel to reel, 8-track, and cassette recorders. They also sold musical instruments and sound modification tools, as well as test equipment.

> 那次去听力诊所之后，我开始更加关注声音而非电路。我会在我初中附近一家名为 Lafayette Radio Electronics 的商店购物。他们不仅出售电子元件和工具，还提供一整套音频设备。他们库存了各种无线电接收器和发射器——包括业余无线电、民用波段、短波、警用扫描仪以及甚高频。店内设有一间试听室，你可以在这里听到不同的家庭立体声组合，后来还有四声道（4通道）扬声器和功放系统。另一个区域展示了公共广播系统，即用于在人群聚集场所扩散声音的扬声器和功放组合，以及麦克风。还有一个区域摆放着录音设备——开盘式录音机、八轨录音机和卡式录音机。他们还销售乐器和音效修改工具，以及测试设备。

I started spending as much time as I could in those other parts of the store. I soon learned the clerks in the more expensive parts of the store were skeptical of my presence. But I figured out that the clerks in the more expensive parts of the store would be much more likely to engage with me if I asked them where the electronic section was first, then came back carrying a bag—which let them know I'd purchased something. When they did engage, I'd have them give me all the literature they had about equipment in their sections to read at home. And whenever possible, I'd surreptitiously listen when the clerks demonstrated, or the customers tried out, a piece of gear.

> 我开始尽可能多地花时间在商店的其他区域。很快我就发现，在商店更昂贵区域的店员对我的出现持怀疑态度。但我发现，如果我先问他们电子产品区在哪里，然后拿着一个袋子回来——让他们知道我买了东西——那么这些区域的店员更有可能与我互动。当他们真的与我互动时，我会让他们给我所有关于他们区域设备的资料，带回家阅读。只要有可能，我会偷偷听店员演示或顾客试用设备时的对话。

My Dad had a significant degree of hearing, and full speaking, capacity. My mother, on the other hand, did not. She didn't use her ears to listen, and, in conversations, she only used her mouth for peak interjections. She spoke with her hands and listened with her eyes and body. My parents first met at the South Carolina School for the Deaf and Blind. Their tight-knit set of friends had all gone there as well. Our families, the D/deaf parents and all of their kids, would go on picnics or to the beach together. I never thought of my parents or their friends as disabled because they could do everything everybody else did. They just did it differently. One of those things was dance. Their community would throw dance parties where everyone would have a great time.

> 我的父亲有相当程度的听力和完整的说话能力。而我的母亲则不然。她不用耳朵听，在对话中，她只用嘴巴进行关键的插话。她用手说话，用眼睛和身体倾听。我的父母第一次见面是在南卡罗来纳州聋哑学校。他们关系紧密的朋友圈也都来自那里。我们的家庭——聋哑父母和他们的所有孩子——会一起去野餐或海滩。我从不认为我的父母或他们的朋友是残疾人，因为他们能做其他人能做的一切事情。只是他们做的方式不同。其中一件事就是跳舞。他们的社区会举办舞会，每个人都会玩得很开心。

All the D/deaf people I've come across have some sort of aesthetic relationship with music. That relationship includes their relationship with others. On the dance floor, they use context clues and nonverbal communication to determine when and how to move. It's important for fully able people to note that our experience can't be extrapolated to people with different ability sets and vice versa. D/deaf people enjoy life the way they enjoy it, not the way we imagine they enjoy it. Watching them dance is pretty amazing if you know they're D/deaf. If no one told you they don't hear music in the standard way, you'd just note in passing that you'd seen some better-than-average dancers. How do D/deaf people hear music? My experience was that my mom would hear it the same way she heard when I banged on the table, or when something fell in the house. She would feel the vibration in her body. My father, who for a long time could hear words clearly but probably couldn't distinguish the musical accompaniment, had a different experience. As he lost his ability to distinguish words, his experience changed.

> 所有我遇到过的 D/deaf 人士都与音乐有着某种审美上的联系。这种联系包括他们与他人的互动。在舞池中，他们利用情境线索和非语言交流来判断何时以及如何移动。健全人士需要明白，我们的体验不能推广到具有不同能力的人身上，反之亦然。D/deaf 人士以他们自己的方式享受生活，而不是我们想象中他们享受生活的方式。如果你知道他们是 D/deaf，看他们跳舞会非常令人惊叹。如果没人告诉你他们不以标准方式听音乐，你只会顺便注意到你看到了一些高于平均水平的舞者。D/deaf 人士如何听音乐？我的体验是，我妈妈会像听到我敲桌子或房子里有东西掉落时那样听音乐。她会在身体中感受到振动。我的父亲，在很长一段时间里能清晰听到话语，但可能无法区分音乐伴奏，有着不同的体验。随着他失去区分话语的能力，他的体验也发生了变化。

As my teen years passed, I became more and more interested in music. I especially became interested in music that moved people, that made people dance. As I got deeper into that, I got more and more interested in bass, in low frequencies. That fed my interest in the tools used for sound reproduction. I noticed that my parents' two-speaker stereo system was actually capable of producing four channels of sound. Soon after, I added two more speakers to my parents' setup. A while later I added a subwoofer, a speaker designed specifically to reproduce low-frequency sounds, as well.

> 随着我进入青少年时期，我对音乐越来越感兴趣。我尤其对那些能打动人心、让人跳舞的音乐感兴趣。随着我深入探索，我对低音和低频越来越感兴趣。这激发了我对声音再现工具的兴趣。我注意到我父母的双扬声器立体声系统实际上能够产生四个声道的声音。不久后，我在父母的设备上又增加了两个扬声器。又过了一段时间，我还增加了一个低音炮，这是一种专门用于再现低频声音的扬声器。

My father loved the subwoofer because it allowed him to distinguish the bass frequencies that were outside the range of his hearing aid. The music I listened to most in the house, funk music and reggae, are both forms of music where movement in the lower frequencies is what drives the music. This movement can come from a bass guitar, a synthesizer (the electronic audio signal–generating musical instrument), or both. As the years went on, he became a big fan of hip-hop. Synthesizers focused on producing percussive sounds—drum machines—are integral to the sound of that music. One sound in particular rose to ubiquity as the genre became the dominant form of popular music, and it remains so today. It's the sound that rattles cars, the boom you hear in the streets and in the clubs. I'm talking about 808s.

> 我父亲很喜欢低音炮，因为它能让他分辨出助听器无法覆盖的低频。我在家里听得最多的音乐是放克音乐和雷鬼音乐，这两种音乐形式中，低频的律动正是驱动音乐的核心。这种律动可以来自低音吉他、合成器（一种产生电子音频信号的乐器），或者两者兼有。随着时间推移，他成了嘻哈音乐的忠实粉丝。专注于产生打击乐声音的合成器——鼓机——是这种音乐声音不可或缺的一部分。当嘻哈成为主流流行音乐形式时，有一种声音变得无处不在，并且至今依然如此。那就是让汽车震动的声响，你在街头和俱乐部里听到的轰鸣。我说的是 808 鼓机的声音。

808s take their name from the piece of gear that first gave us their sound, the Roland TR-808 drum machine. The TR-808 was one of the first dedicated percussion synthesizers that allowed users to create their own rhythmic patterns. Unlike the other programmable drum machines of its day, the LinnDrum and the Oberheim DMX, which used recordings of drum sounds as their sound source, the TR-808 used electronic synthesis. Roland's machine was the least popular one back then, so the company stopped making them after about two years.
The bass drum on the TR-808 drum machine is synthesized using a short pulse to start the sound, then a long sustained sound. The sustained part of the sound on the original TR-808 is a 56 Hz sine wave, a pure and simple bass tone. Beatmakers, people who make the music (a.k.a. beats) that rappers rhyme over, discovered that if they used the TR-808 bass drum together with drums sampled from records, the 808s would give the music a powerful low-frequency energy, a boom.

> ==808s 的名称来源于最早赋予它们声音的设备，即 Roland TR-808 鼓机。TR-808 是最早的专用打击乐合成器之一，允许用户创建自己的节奏模式。与当时其他可编程鼓机（如 LinnDrum 和 Oberheim DMX）不同，这些鼓机使用录制鼓声作为音源，而 TR-808 则采用电子合成。Roland 的这款机器在当时最不受欢迎，因此公司在大约两年后停止了生产。==TR-808 鼓机上的低音鼓通过一个短脉冲启动声音，然后是一个长持续音。原始 TR-808 的持续音部分是一个 56 Hz 的正弦波，这是一种纯粹而简单的低音音调。节拍制作人（即制作音乐（又称节拍）供说唱歌手押韵的人）发现，如果他们将 TR-808 低音鼓与从唱片中采样的鼓声结合使用，808s 能为音乐带来强大的低频能量，即一种轰鸣声。

Beatmakers soon figured out that they could extend their ability to use the 808 sound by using their sampler to record it. People started using the sound to make bass parts, which created a new sonic texture. Sound designers have focused a lot of attention on 808s, and the sound continues to morph. Today 808s, like the lowest drums in various forms of West African music, often function as rhythm instruments and harmonic instruments simultaneously.

> Beatmakers 很快发现，他们可以通过使用采样器录制 808 声音来扩展使用该声音的能力。人们开始使用这种声音制作低音部分，从而创造出一种新的声音质感。音效设计师对 808 音色投入了大量关注，而这种声音也在不断演变。如今，808 音色如同西非音乐中各种形式的低音鼓一样，常常同时充当节奏乐器和和声乐器。

Like the 808 defined the sound of hip-hop, even before the development of electronic synthesis, the Hammond organ became one of the defining sounds of gospel music, a form that blossomed in Black churches while the blues was developing in Black clubs. An electromechanical keyboard synthesizer, the Hammond uses mechanical means to generate sine wave–like tones, delivered in a range from 32.69 to 2092.3 Hz, that are combined to create complex musical tones. The organ was in wide use in churches and clubs in the United States. The Hammond is commonly used in combination with the Leslie speaker, which is an integrated amplifier/speaker that can deliver all of the frequencies a Hammond organ produces. In fact, that combo was designed for use in churches, and the instrument maintained its popularity there even after electronic synthesizers became the norm.

> 正如 808 定义了嘻哈音乐的声音一样，甚至在电子合成技术发展之前，Hammond organ 就成为了福音音乐的决定性声音之一——福音音乐是在 Black churches 中蓬勃发展的一种音乐形式，而蓝调则在 Black clubs 中发展。Hammond 是一种机电式键盘合成器，它通过机械方式生成类似正弦波的音调，频率范围从 32.69 Hz 到 2092.3 Hz，这些音调组合起来创造出复杂的音乐音色。这种风琴在美国的教堂和俱乐部中被广泛使用。Hammond 通常与 Leslie speaker 配合使用，后者是一种集成式放大器/扬声器，能够传递 Hammond organ 产生的所有频率。事实上，这种组合设计用于教堂，并且即使在电子合成器成为主流之后，该乐器在那里仍保持着流行。

Gospel musicians are often top-quality players. I had some cousins, the older ones my father's age and their son who was around my age, who were the music ministers for LaGree Baptist Church, which was on Harlem's main street, 125th Street. They were definitely top quality. That church is no longer there, but the building had been a movie theater before LaGree's congregation repurposed it. The musicians, sometimes just an organist and a drummer, other times four-piece bands that included electric guitar and bass, and sometimes electric piano as well, would set up in between the choir and the deacons. Once in a while, vocal trios or quartets accompanied solely by an electric guitarist would give musical ministry. The somewhat archaic sound of those was, in its own subtle way, for me as moving as the more modern sound of the choir plus band.

> 福音音乐家通常是顶级水平的乐手。我有一些表亲，其中年长的与我父亲同龄，还有他们的儿子，和我年纪相仿，他们是 LaGree Baptist Church 的音乐事工，这座教堂位于哈莱姆区的主街 125th Street 上。他们绝对是顶级水准。那座教堂如今已不复存在，但那栋建筑在 LaGree 会众重新利用之前曾是一家电影院。乐手们——有时只有一位管风琴手和一位鼓手，有时是包含电吉他和贝斯的四人乐队，有时还加上电钢琴——会在唱诗班和执事之间布置乐器。偶尔，仅由一位电吉他手伴奏的声乐三重唱或四重唱会带来音乐事工。那些略显古朴的声音，以其微妙的方式，对我来说与更现代的唱诗班加乐队组合同样动人。

My cousin-in-law, the organist's mother who was a keyboard player herself, was not quite as proficient. But she had discovered something that became her secret weapon. She had figured out that one of the low-frequency tones that the Hammond organ delivered was the resonant frequency of the building that housed the church. When she stepped on the pedal to hit that note, the building would vibrate in response. That building would thunder and boom, evoking a feeling of awe and power in the church's congregation. Her use of low-frequency sine waves foreshadowed the car and building rattling boom of the 808 bass drum by decades. When a building I'm in gets rattled by 808s blasting out of a passing car, I think of her.

> 我的表亲妻子，那位管风琴手的母亲，她本人也是一位键盘手，但技艺并不那么精湛。然而，她发现了一样东西，成了她的秘密武器。她意识到 Hammond 管风琴发出的一个低频音调正好是教堂所在建筑的共振频率。当她踩下踏板奏出那个音符时，整栋建筑会随之振动。那栋建筑会雷鸣般轰响，在教堂会众中唤起敬畏和力量之感。她对低频正弦波的运用，比那种让汽车和建筑震响的 808 低音鼓的轰鸣早了几十年。当我所在的大楼被路过汽车传来的 808 鼓声震得格格作响时，我就会想起她。

Using a building's resonance as a musical tool is the sort of thing people normally associate with high art. Some of us would think of it as experimental. Others might think of it as snooty, still others as pretentious, and a significant fraction of those who heard about it would think of it as both. But, in the current state of American culture, you wouldn't think about it as something that happened every week at a church in Harlem in the 1970s that was done to move people in a spiritual way. When Kool & the Gang sang about being scientists of sound, they were talking about exactly the sort of discovery and subsequent manipulation my cousin's mom made. They were talking about using the things we've learned to measure to intervene in the parts of our existence that are resistant to measure. They were talking about melding tools and working methods that have taken years, if not decades, to develop and codify to a context that at its most controlled allows codification while resisting it and in all cases challenges it.

> 利用建筑物的共振作为音乐工具，通常是人们联想到高雅艺术的事情。有些人会认为这很实验性，有些人可能觉得它傲慢，还有人觉得它做作，而听说过的人中相当一部分会认为它两者兼有。但在美国文化的现状下，你不会想到这在 1970 年代哈莱姆区的一座教堂里每周都会发生，而且目的是以精神方式触动人们。当 Kool & the Gang 唱到自己是声音科学家时，他们谈论的正是我表姐的母亲所做出的那种发现和后续操作。他们谈论的是利用我们已学会测量的东西，去干预我们存在中那些难以测量的部分。他们谈论的是将那些花费了多年（甚至数十年）发展和编纂的工具与工作方法，融合到一个在受控状态下允许编纂、同时又抵制编纂、并且在所有情况下都挑战它的语境中。

If we say Black music is about anything, we have to say it's about the stories of Black people. Black people are usually presented as existing according to a small set of archetypal forms, and our creativity is distilled down to a few stereotypes acceptable to popular culture. But the experiences of the full range of Black people and the interplay of the entirety of the solutions they develop while living was, is, and will continue to be necessary to develop a culture rich and varied enough to continually adapt to the conditions Black people, both as a group and individuals, are faced with. I often say there's more than one way to be Black. The wide range of our experiences is the frame for the multiplicity of ideas that fuel Black musical creativity.

> 如果说黑人音乐有什么核心主题，那一定是关于黑人的故事。黑人通常被呈现为以少数几种原型形式存在，我们的创造力被提炼为流行文化所能接受的几个刻板印象。但各种黑人的经历，以及他们在生活中所发展出的全部解决方案的相互作用，过去、现在和将来都是发展一种足够丰富多样的文化所必需的，这种文化要能不断适应黑人群体和个体所面临的条件。我常说，成为黑人的方式不止一种。我们经历的广泛多样性，正是推动黑人音乐创造力的多元思想的框架。
