## 1  Sticking It to the Man
***Early Neoliberalism in Korean Pop Music***
**1  对抗建制：*韩国流行音乐中的早期新自由主义***

> ROALD MALIANGKAY

### Open Competition
**公开竞赛**

In cultural studies, as well as in many other disciplines, the use of the term "neoliberalism" has become increasingly prevalent. Outside the field of economics, to which it was once tied more closely, it has become a blanket term for capitalist measures that include deregulation, reductions in public expenditures, and an emphasis on individual entrepreneurship, competition, and free trade. Terry Flew notes that when interpreted as an approach to economic policy, it may be endorsed by left-leaning policy makers due to its inherent goal to reduce the cost of bureaucracy, but it is more frequently associated with ruthless approaches to capitalist gain.[^1] Nick Couldry argues that neoliberal strategies pose a danger when they promote a worldview that regards markets as the ideal sociopolitical form of organization and allows its principles to drown out alternative criteria. While neoliberalism may, for example, appear to foreground the voices of individuals, including those of fan communities, he warns that "the notion of freedom underlying neoliberalism is abstracted from any understanding of the social processes that underpin 'voice' in its full sense as an embodied process of effective speech." [^2] In the workplace, the corporate identity does indeed risk obfuscating truly individual, personal experience and value, and while one might posit that the self-branding so common in popular culture is a triumph of individual expression, Couldry contends that it merely represents the "opportunity to compete as a commodity."[^3] Since neoliberal values have become internalized and very much part of our culture, championing a more introspective and less performative account of the non-market-driven needs and emotions of individuals may prove quite challenging.

> 在文化研究以及许多其他学科中，“新自由主义”一词的使用变得越来越普遍。在经济学领域之外（该词曾与经济学联系更为紧密），它已成为涵盖放松管制、削减公共支出、强调个人创业精神、竞争和自由贸易等资本主义措施的统称。Terry Flew 指出，当被解释为一种经济政策方法时，它可能得到左倾政策制定者的支持，因为其内在目标是降低官僚成本，但它更常与资本主义获利的冷酷手段联系在一起。[^1] Nick Couldry 认为，当新自由主义策略推广一种将市场视为理想社会政治组织形式的世界观，并允许其原则淹没其他替代标准时，就会带来危险。例如，尽管新自由主义可能看似突出了个人（包括粉丝社群）的声音，但他警告说，“新自由主义所依据的自由概念，脱离了对其完整意义上作为有效话语的具体化过程的‘声音’所依托的社会过程的任何理解。”[^2] 在工作场所，企业身份确实有可能混淆真正个人的、私人的经历和价值，虽然有人可能认为流行文化中如此常见的个人品牌化是个人表达的胜利，但 Couldry 认为，这仅仅代表了“作为商品进行竞争的机会”。[^3] 由于新自由主义价值观已被内化并成为我们文化的重要组成部分，倡导对个人非市场驱动的需求和情感进行更内省、更少表演性的描述，可能会被证明是相当具有挑战性的。

The business of Korean popular music is driven by neoliberalism in a range of different ways. According to Inkyu Kang, it is responsible for the hypercommodification and hyperrationalization of K-pop, as evidenced by the creation of "talents" through years of training, the wide range of spinoff products, and the frequent use of lip-synching. And it explains, he argues, the deindividualization of group members and their replaceability within idol formations.[^4] But neoliberalism can be identified in another equally important aspect of K-pop, namely the semantics of K-pop performances. In conformist and (from a Korean point of view) politically correct fashion,[^5] many idol formations passionately endorse the voices of their fans through messages of individuality, girl power, and self-love. The phenomenon of Seo Taiji and Boys demonstrated,[^6] however, that emphasis on personal empowerment and symbols of nonconformity can be used to promote major brands, including idol formations, luring fans into portraying themselves as critical consumers who happen to have developed a "bias." There is one other realm where the connection between neoliberalism and today's K-pop industry is predominant. It is where competition and public scrutiny allow individuals a chance to break out as rising stars through the reality talent show.

> 韩国流行音乐产业在多个层面上受到新自由主义的驱动。据 Inkyu Kang 指出，这种驱动体现在 K-pop 的过度商品化和过度理性化上，例如通过多年培训打造“人才”、开发种类繁多的衍生品以及频繁使用假唱。他认为，这还解释了偶像团体成员的去个性化以及他们在偶像组合中的可替代性。[^4] 但新自由主义在 K-pop 的另一个同样重要的方面也有所体现，即 K-pop 表演的语义层面。许多偶像团体以顺应主流且（从韩国视角看）政治正确的方式，[^5] 通过传递个性、女性力量和自爱的信息，热情地支持粉丝的声音。然而，Seo Taiji and Boys 的现象表明，[^6] 对个人赋权和非从众象征的强调，可以被用来推广包括偶像团体在内的大品牌，诱使粉丝将自己描绘成恰好发展出某种“偏好”的批判性消费者。还有另一个领域，新自由主义与当今 K-pop 产业之间的联系尤为突出。在这个领域中，竞争和公众审视让个体有机会通过真人选秀节目脱颖而出，成为新星。

Over the past decade, reality talent shows have experienced a dramatic surge in popularity on South Korean TV networks. Examples include Mnet's Superstar K (2009–2016), The Voice of Korea (2012–2013), I Can See Your Voice (Neo-ui moksori-ga boyeo, since 2017), and Produce 101 (2016–2019); tvN's Korea's Got Talent (2011–2012); SBS's Survival Audition K-Pop Star (2011–2017); KBS's Top Band (2011–2015) and Singing Battle (Norae ssaum, 2016–2017); and MBC's Star Audition: The Great Birth (Seuta odisyeon widaehan tansaeng, 2010–2013) and I Am a Singer (Na-neun gasuda, 2011–2015). Even Marvel's comic book character Luna Snow, the K-pop idol turned superhero, was introduced in 2019 as a member of 4L1T, "winners of the idol origins contest." [^7] Reality talent shows revolve around a small panel of noted individuals judging music performances by amateurs over the course of competitive elimination rounds. As they play into the general public's distrust of the music industry's manipulative power and gatekeeping, viewers are asked to cast their vote, either live or online. Meanwhile, the amateur status of contestants and the name of a show like The Voice of Korea fuel the sentiment that audiences are playing a role in curating true talent.

> 在过去的十年里，真人选秀节目在韩国电视网络上的受欢迎程度急剧上升。例如 Mnet 的《Superstar K》（2009–2016 年）、《韩国好声音》（2012–2013 年）、《看见你的声音》（Neo-ui moksori-ga boyeo，2017 年至今）和《Produce 101》（2016–2019 年）；tvN 的《韩国达人秀》（2011–2012 年）；SBS 的《生存 audition K-Pop Star》（2011–2017 年）；KBS 的《顶级乐队》（2011–2015 年）和《歌唱对决》（Norae ssaum，2016–2017 年）；以及 MBC 的《明星 audition：伟大的诞生》（Seuta odisyeon widaehan tansaeng，2010–2013 年）和《我是歌手》（Na-neun gasuda，2011–2015 年）。甚至漫威漫画角色 Luna Snow，这位从 K-pop 偶像转型为超级英雄的角色，也在 2019 年作为 4L1T 的成员被引入，该组合是“偶像起源大赛的获胜者”。[^7] 真人选秀节目围绕一小群知名人士组成评审团，在竞争性淘汰赛过程中评判业余选手的音乐表演。由于这些节目利用了公众对音乐产业操控力和把关行为的不信任，观众被要求通过现场或在线方式投票。与此同时，参赛者的业余身份以及像《韩国好声音》这样的节目名称，强化了观众在发掘真正才华方面发挥作用的情绪。

Hyeonu Han argues that the talent shows are more competitions than auditions. He believes an audition is a way of seeing how well you fit a particular role, while a competition focuses on how well a predetermined song is played: "The main concern is who sings the designated song better and who goes up to the higher pitch." [^8] But although the element of competition is undeniable and will also play out among the fans, the shows equally encourage the audience to assess whether a candidate is "fit" to win. As important as their voices, backstories serve as evidence of contestants' authenticity. Critics note that viewers are made to judge a limited range of renditions of well-established styles of music based on vague criteria.[^9] Indeed, for the television shows to be successful, and for the successful contestants to earn the lucrative rewards that will allow them to commit themselves fully to their artistic ambitions, the undefined criteria for performance cannot completely shun stylistic conventions or be perceived as superimposed; the contestants must emulate existing conventions to win the maximum number of votes. The business of popular entertainment favors those with the greatest commercial promise, and despite the outcome arguably being predetermined, by seemingly defying the cultural hegemony the notion of raw talent promotes sales. As Jeremy Gilbert notes, the idea of the "innate talent" of individuals serves to "justify the obviously selfperpetuating nature of inegalitarian institutions and social relations." [^10] Adorno's idea of pseudo-individualism therefore applies to both the business model and the supposed talent of the contestants.[^11] The considerable commercial importance of the shows – some have generated viewer ratings as high as 20 percent – can cast doubts upon the validity of the polls. After all, judges and producers may have a vested interest in selecting contestants who, for better or worse, appeal to a large percentage of the population. When in spite of the format's popularity and reliance on viewer voting, Mnet's producers feared that the 2019 installment of their Produce series would produce an unfavorable outcome, they rigged the voting process.[^12]

> Hyeonu Han 认为，这些选秀节目更像是竞赛而非试镜。他相信，试镜是一种考察你如何契合特定角色的方式，而竞赛则侧重于如何完美演绎一首既定歌曲：“主要关注点是谁能更好地演唱指定歌曲，谁能唱到更高的音调。” [^8] 然而，尽管竞争元素不可否认，并且也会在粉丝之间展开，但节目同样鼓励观众评估候选人是否“适合”获胜。与他们的嗓音同样重要的是，幕后故事作为选手真实性的证据。评论家指出，观众被要求基于模糊的标准来评判有限范围内、风格成熟的音乐演绎。[^9] 确实，为了让电视节目成功，并让成功的选手获得丰厚的回报，从而能够全身心投入艺术抱负，表演中未明确的标准不能完全回避风格惯例，也不能被视为强加于人；选手必须模仿现有惯例，以赢得最多票数。流行娱乐产业青睐那些最具商业潜力的人，尽管结果可能被认为是预先确定的，但通过看似挑战文化霸权，原始天赋的概念促进了销售。正如 Jeremy Gilbert 所指出的，个人“天生才能”的观念旨在“为不平等制度和社会关系中明显自我延续的本质辩护。” [^10] 因此，Adorno 的伪个人主义理念既适用于商业模式，也适用于选手所谓的才能。这些节目巨大的商业重要性——有些节目的收视率高达 20%——可能使投票的有效性受到质疑。毕竟，评委和制作人可能在选择那些无论好坏都能吸引大多数观众的选手时，拥有既得利益。尽管这种形式很受欢迎且依赖观众投票，但当 Mnet 的制作人担心其 2019 年的《Produce》系列会产生不利结果时，他们操纵了投票过程。[^12]

John Fiske defines fandom as a minority's act of appropriating "certain performers, narratives or genres" from mainstream pop. "It is," he finds, "typically associated with the cultural tastes of subordinated formations of the people, particularly with those disempowered by any combination of gender, age, class and race." [^13] The definition is helpful because it identifies the crucial factor of empowerment, which may take different forms and could be taken to comprise cultural, social, and economic involvement or recognition. Although K-pop and talent shows have come under increasing scrutiny by fans, the criticism does not immediately pose a challenge to the business model per se. Critics may genuinely oppose aspects of the industry's hegemony and have a significant effect on sales but may ultimately be driven by a desire to find unity in a common purpose, promote their fandom and ability to scrutinize, or compete with fans of other idols. Since fandom presents an avenue for young people to distinguish themselves, even strong criticism of talent shows may be geared less toward generating systemic change than toward generating likes. Pop culture fandom is aspirational; it yields greater agency to the subordinated, even though the majority of critically engaged fans may not seek control over particular media to reverse their subordination, however loud their voices.

> John Fiske 将粉丝文化定义为少数群体从主流流行文化中“挪用特定表演者、叙事或类型”的行为。他指出，“这通常与从属群体（尤其是那些因性别、年龄、阶级和种族的某种组合而被剥夺权力的人）的文化品味相关联。” [^13] 这一定义很有帮助，因为它指出了赋权这一关键因素，这种赋权可能以不同形式呈现，并可能包含文化、社会和经济层面的参与或认可。尽管 K-pop 和选秀节目日益受到粉丝的审视，但这种批评本身并未立即对商业模式构成挑战。批评者可能确实反对该行业霸权中的某些方面，并对销量产生显著影响，但其最终动机可能是希望在共同目标中寻求团结、提升自身粉丝群体和审视能力，或与其他偶像的粉丝竞争。由于粉丝文化为年轻人提供了脱颖而出的途径，即便对选秀节目的强烈批评，其目的也可能更倾向于获取点赞，而非推动系统性变革。流行文化粉丝文化具有进取性；它为从属群体赋予了更强的能动性，尽管大多数积极参与批评的粉丝可能并不寻求通过控制特定媒体来扭转其从属地位——无论他们的声音多么响亮。

### Selling Records
**唱片售卖**

In the first few decades of the twentieth century, record companies' primary source of revenue was the sale of physical copies. The wonders of new media technologies and the foreign scenes and sounds they produced certainly drew in affluent consumers, but as soon as prices began to drop and the size of the middle class began to grow, the business became a dominant feature of daily life. In Korea, too, talent shows, radio broadcasts, and countless advertisements promoting the latest records could be found everywhere. While the elements of cost, sound quality, and uniqueness remained considerable selling points for some time, the growing phenomenon of celebrity stardom eventually turned fandom into a fourth major factor driving sales. Enjoying the accolades of connoisseurship and cosmopolitanism that their involvement awarded, many young urbanites kept up to date on the activities of their idols and emulated their style. They bought magazines and frequented record shops and popular music venues to follow the latest developments and share experiences and opinions.

> 在二十世纪最初的几十年里，唱片公司的主要收入来源是实体唱片的销售。新媒体技术的奇迹以及它们所呈现的异国场景和声音，无疑吸引了富裕的消费者，但一旦价格开始下降、中产阶级规模开始扩大，这项业务便成为日常生活中的主导特征。在韩国，才艺秀、广播节目以及无数推广最新唱片的广告也随处可见。尽管成本、音质和独特性这些因素在一段时间内仍然是重要的卖点，但名人明星现象的日益兴起最终将粉丝追捧转变为推动销售的第四大因素。许多年轻的城市居民享受着参与其中所带来的鉴赏力和世界主义的光环，他们紧跟偶像的动态，并模仿他们的风格。他们购买杂志，频繁光顾唱片店和流行音乐场所，以追踪最新发展并分享经验和观点。

Having steadily grown an interest in Korean talent among Korean and Japanese middle-class consumers, in the late 1920s the companies began to increasingly bring out modern, faster arrangements of traditional songs called "new folk songs" (sin minyo) and yuhaengga (yuhaeng, popular; ga, song). Because they followed Japan's pentatonic yonanuki scale, they could become popular with both Koreans and Japanese domestically, opening up the possibility of a Japanese-language release. Even so, in 1928, F. H. Goldsmith, a recording engineer for Victor Japan, said that across northeast Asia people were primarily interested in their own music, with native pop genres generally holding little commercial promise overseas.[^14] Jazz was the last of the three primary pop song styles to emerge and thrive. Unlike the other two styles, which could be described as melancholic, jazz songs were mostly upbeat. Because many of them corresponded with passionate dance sequences admired on the silver screen, they felt decidedly more modern and, on account of their more playful character, may have symbolized conformity to the political status quo. Indeed, Yongwoo Lee finds that in Korea's context, young people turned jazz music into a commodity that tied them to America's utopic contemporaneity.[^15] Rather than merely emulating aspects of Western culture, however, Koreans sought to own it. To a Westerner, Korean jazz may have seemed flattering, but to Koreans it was defiantly progressive; its very existence denied foreign patency and allowed it to be embedded with Korean sensibilities. Despite having no control over any particular medium or genre, Koreans were able to create "glocalized" forms of entertainment that highlighted their own potential and expressed uniquely Korean sentiments. But while Korean successes overseas were important for their self-image, Korean jazz did not sell abroad and was not even popular with Korea's own elite, who preferred Western classical music.[^16]

> 在 20 世纪 20 年代末，随着韩国和日本中产阶级消费者对韩国人才的兴趣稳步增长，各公司开始越来越多地推出名为“新民谣”（sin minyo）和流行歌（yuhaengga，yuhaeng 意为流行，ga 意为歌曲）的传统歌曲的现代、节奏更快的改编版本。由于这些歌曲遵循日本五声音阶的 yonanuki 音阶，它们能够在韩国和日本国内都受到欢迎，从而为发行日语版本打开了可能性。即便如此，在 1928 年，Victor Japan 的录音工程师 F. H. Goldsmith 表示，在整个东北亚，人们主要对自己国家的音乐感兴趣，本土流行音乐类型在海外通常几乎没有商业前景。[^14] 爵士乐是第三种主要流行歌曲风格，也是最后出现并蓬勃发展的。与其他两种可被描述为忧郁的风格不同，爵士歌曲大多节奏欢快。由于其中许多歌曲与银幕上备受赞赏的激情舞蹈场景相呼应，它们显得明显更加现代，并且因其更具玩乐性质的特点，可能象征着对政治现状的顺从。事实上，Yongwoo Lee 发现，在韩国的背景下，年轻人将爵士乐变成了一种将他们与美国乌托邦式的当代性联系起来的商品。[^15] 然而，韩国人并不仅仅是模仿西方文化的某些方面，而是试图将其据为己有。对西方人来说，韩国爵士乐可能显得讨好，但对韩国人而言，它却是一种大胆进步的象征；它的存在本身就否定了外国的支配地位，并使其能够融入韩国人的情感。尽管韩国人无法控制任何特定的媒介或流派，但他们能够创造出“全球本土化”的娱乐形式，凸显自身的潜力，并表达独特的韩国情感。然而，尽管韩国在海外的成功对其自我形象至关重要，但韩国爵士乐并未在海外销售，甚至在本国精英阶层中也不受欢迎，他们更偏爱西方古典音乐。[^16]

By the early 1930s, the primary companies competing for a slice of the Korean music market were Columbia and Victor, closely followed in terms of market share by Chieron, Polydor, Okeh, and Taepyeong (Taihei in Japanese). I estimate their total annual income to have hovered around 35 million won around the mid-1930s, equivalent to approximately US$350 million today. In a discussion with noted pop singers and record company representatives in January 1936, Samcheolli (Our land) magazine editor Donghwan Kim claimed that sales of traditional Korean music ("Joseon soripan") accounted for a third of the total volume of 1.5 million records sold,[^17] which suggests that until the early 1940s, when the production of records collapsed, the Korean market was dominated by "new folk songs" and yuhaengga, jazz songs, and Western classical music. Korean pop music accounted for more than half of record sales, and some pop songs, whether foreign or Korean, sold thousands of copies almost overnight. But like today, due to the high cost of production, record companies rarely took chances; although nudity had long proven to sell live acts regardless of artistic talent, gramophone recordings had to ideally be sold before they ended up on the shelves.

> 到 1930 年代初期，争夺韩国音乐市场份额的主要公司是 Columbia 和 Victor，紧随其后的是 Chieron、Polydor、Okeh 和 Taepyeong（日语中为 Taihei）。我估计它们在 1930 年代中期左右的年总收入徘徊在 3500 万韩元，相当于今天的约 3.5 亿美元。在 1936 年 1 月与著名流行歌手和唱片公司代表的一次讨论中，《三千里》杂志编辑金东焕声称，传统韩国音乐（“朝鲜唱盘”）的销量占 150 万张唱片总销量的三分之一，[^17] 这表明直到 1940 年代初期唱片生产崩溃之前，韩国市场一直由“新民谣”和流行歌、爵士歌曲以及西方古典音乐主导。韩国流行音乐占唱片销量的一半以上，一些流行歌曲，无论是外国还是韩国的，几乎一夜之间就能卖出数千张。但和今天一样，由于制作成本高昂，唱片公司很少冒险；尽管长期以来，裸体表演已被证明能吸引现场观众，无论艺术才华如何，但留声机唱片最好在上架前就卖出去。

Music fandom grew considerably in the 1930s, but few contemporary publications discussed the phenomenon in detail. The first use of the English term "fan" (paen) in regard to admirers of pop culture in Korea may be in the Japanese-language paper Pusan nippō from June 1925, which reported that fans in Busan, presumably all Japanese, were excited about the arrival of actress Makino Teruko.[^18] Another, from December 1926, referred to Korean followers of Hollywood star Rudolph Valentino.[^19] The term "fan" retained its association with things foreign for some time and was still placed between quotation marks in an editorial on popular music a decade later.[^20] While it was used to refer to sports fans, too, when it was applied to popular music, not a realm dominated by men, it appears to have carried negative connotations. The many complaints by Koreans about the ill effects of modern urban culture in the early 1930s show that some believed pop music was having a druglike effect on Korean youth, with gramophone technology often receiving partial blame. In an editorial from January 1930, for example, an impression of an evening at Café Baron in Seoul reads: "When the sound of jazz belches from a gramophone player, waitresses twitch their shoulders and wriggle their bums. These unsightly mechanical rhythms are springing up everywhere." [^21] In a piece on the effect of pop music on children published four years later, a critic wrote: "When I walk down an alley, I often hear a phonograph playing. The lyrics are usually quite meaningless and messy." [^22] And in 1935, folklorist Seokha Song lamented that records with "empty pop songs" (pyetoehan yuhaengga) were now playing virtually everywhere around the country.[^23] It is ironic that criticism of the medium took decades to come to the fore, and that when it finally did, due of course in part to the emergence of print media – in the 1920s and 1930s important platforms for the discussion of popular culture included the magazines Samcheolli, Byeolgeongon (Another world), and Jogwang (Dawn) – Koreans were playing a greater role in the record industry than ever before. Not only were they increasingly involved in the production of records both behind the scenes and in front of the microphone, but even the voice of Korean fans had become difficult to ignore: Many wrote letters to their idols, followed them around after performances, sent gifts like cosmetics or chocolate, and they sometimes demanded that the radio play their favorite music.[^24] Indeed, in January 1940, pop singer Song Geumnyeong acknowledged that "today's fans of popular songs (yuhaengga) have certainly become more knowledgeable and better at their hobby." [^25] What is more, despite growing criticism of the involvement of gisaeng (young hostesses or courtesans) in popular culture, which extended even to their use of fashion accessories,[^26] record companies and the radio provided talented ones with better career opportunities than they had ever had.

> 音乐粉丝群体在 1930 年代显著增长，但当时的出版物鲜有详细讨论这一现象。英语术语“fan”（paen）在韩国首次用于指代流行文化爱好者，可能出现在 1925 年 6 月的日文报纸《釜山日报》中，该报道称釜山的粉丝（推测均为日本人）对女演员牧野照子的到来感到兴奋。[^18] 另一篇 1926 年 12 月的报道提及了好莱坞明星 Rudolph Valentino 的韩国追随者。[^19] “fan”一词在相当一段时间内保留着与外来事物的关联，甚至在十年后一篇关于流行音乐的社论中仍被置于引号内。[^20] 尽管该词也用于指代体育迷，但应用于并非男性主导领域的流行音乐时，似乎带有负面含义。1930 年代初期韩国人对现代都市文化不良影响的诸多抱怨表明，一些人认为流行音乐对韩国青年具有类似毒品的效果，而留声机技术常被部分归咎。例如，在 1930 年 1 月的一篇社论中，对首尔 Baron 咖啡馆夜晚的印象写道：“当爵士乐声从留声机中喷涌而出时，女侍者们耸动肩膀、扭动臀部。这些不雅的机械节奏正四处蔓延。”[^21] 在四年后一篇关于流行音乐对儿童影响的文章中，一位评论家写道：“当我走过小巷时，常听到留声机播放音乐。歌词通常毫无意义且杂乱无章。”[^22] 1935 年，民俗学者 Seokha Song 哀叹，带有“空洞流行歌曲”的唱片如今几乎在全国各地播放。[^23] 具有讽刺意味的是，对媒介的批评历经数十年才浮出水面，而当它最终出现时——部分原因当然在于印刷媒体的兴起（1920 年代和 1930 年代，讨论流行文化的重要平台包括杂志《三千里》、《别乾坤》和《曙光》）——韩国人在唱片业中扮演的角色比以往任何时候都更为重要。他们不仅越来越多地参与唱片的制作（无论是幕后还是台前），甚至韩国粉丝的声音也变得难以忽视：许多人给偶像写信、在演出后追随他们、赠送化妆品或巧克力等礼物，有时还要求电台播放他们最喜爱的音乐。[^24] 事实上，1940 年 1 月，流行歌手 Song Geumnyeong 承认：“如今流行歌曲的粉丝确实变得更有见识，也更擅长自己的爱好了。”[^25] 更重要的是，尽管对艺妓参与流行文化的批评日益增多——甚至延伸到她们对时尚配饰的使用[^26]——唱片公司和电台为有才华的艺妓提供了比以往更好的职业机会。

### Recognizing Talent
**识别人才**

Newspapers and the radio played a significant role in the promotion of the new repertoires, while fandom and conformity did their part to urge consumers to keep up to date. To be at the forefront of new developments in popular culture, or at the very least aware of them, has always been a major driver of fandom. In May 1933, when portraits of individual artists were regularly featured in ads and on records and lyric sheets, the music label Chieron debuted an anonymous singer simply called Ms. Chieron. It challenged fans to show themselves to be true connoisseurs by identifying the hidden identity of the vocalist. Columbia Records followed suit in September 1934 with a Ms. Korea, whose face on lyric sheets was made unrecognizable by a black banner across her eyes. A Ms. Regal and Mr. Columbia debuted in 1934 and 1935, respectively.[^27] An ad in the Maeil sinbo (Daily report) again a year later also played into the pressure to be in the know. Using small profile pictures of two women, a record shop challenged readers to work out the identity of the popular singers based on a few small hints.[^28] In these and other ways record companies promoted their products widely and aggressively; shoppers, movie audiences, and journal subscribers were all subjected to countless advertisements and promotions. While shop owners did their part distributing flyers and lyric sheets for a new record or gramophone player, record companies and the radio advertised live radio shows and public events where audiences could witness the latest or future stars performing live.

> 报纸和广播在推广新曲目方面发挥了重要作用，而粉丝群体和从众心理则促使消费者紧跟潮流。始终处于流行文化发展的前沿，或者至少对其有所了解，一直是粉丝群体的主要驱动力。1933 年 5 月，当个别艺术家的肖像经常出现在广告、唱片和歌词单上时，音乐厂牌 Chieron 推出了一位仅被称为“Chieron 女士”的匿名歌手。它向粉丝发起挑战，要求他们通过识别这位歌手的隐藏身份来证明自己是真正的鉴赏家。Columbia 唱片公司于 1934 年 9 月效仿，推出了一位“韩国女士”，其歌词单上的脸部因眼睛上的一条黑色横幅而无法辨认。“Regal 女士”和“Columbia 先生”分别于 1934 年和 1935 年首次亮相。[^27] 一年后，《每日新闻》上的一则广告也利用了人们渴望了解内情的压力。一家唱片店使用两位女性的小头像，挑战读者根据一些细微线索找出流行歌手的身份。[^28] 通过这些及其他方式，唱片公司广泛而积极地推广其产品；购物者、电影观众和期刊订阅者都受到无数广告和促销活动的影响。虽然店主在分发新唱片或留声机播放器的传单和歌词单方面尽了自己的一份力，但唱片公司和广播电台则宣传现场广播节目和公共活动，让观众可以亲眼目睹最新或未来的明星现场表演。

Unlike gisaeng, who could rely on their management to provide introductions, talented singers with no ties to the music industry could make a name for themselves by winning a prize in a contest. The idea may not have been adopted from abroad, but if it was, it certainly shows that Korea's entertainment industry followed developments elsewhere. The 1935 Hollywood production Every Night at Eight, about a singing trio trying to break through using an amateur radio hour, was the first to piggyback on the popularity of the amateur hour concept in the United States.[^29] In the United Kingdom; in Japan, where Every Night at Eight opened in April 1936; and in China, too, talent shows were becoming a regular feature on the radio.[^30] In Hong Kong in January 1932, the chairman of the Broadcasting Committee issued an invitation to local talent for a singing contest.[^31] And in Shanghai in November 1935, an amateur contest was organized with the help of several local radio stations under the auspices of the RCA-Victor record company. Running over the course of a week and decided by the listeners based on "popular vote," it sought to support talented contestants and in the process "point to all the stars who began at the bottom." [^32]

> 与妓生不同，她们可以依靠经纪公司提供引荐机会，而与音乐产业毫无关联的才华歌手则可以通过在比赛中获奖来打响名声。这一理念或许并非舶来品，但若确属借鉴，无疑表明韩国娱乐产业紧跟国际潮流。1935 年好莱坞作品《每夜八点》讲述了一个歌唱组合试图通过业余广播时段崭露头角的故事，该片率先借势美国业余时段概念的热潮。[^29] 在英国；在日本（《每夜八点》于 1936 年 4 月上映）；在中国，选秀节目也逐渐成为广播电台的固定内容。[^30] 1932 年 1 月香港广播委员会主席曾向本地人才发出歌唱比赛邀请。[^31] 1935 年 11 月上海则在 RCA-Victor 唱片公司赞助下，联合多家本地电台举办了业余比赛。这场持续一周的比赛由听众通过“普选”决定胜负，旨在扶持参赛人才，并以此“指引所有从底层起步的明星”。[^32]

In Korea, from October 1933, for approximately six months, Columbia Records staged a pop music singing contest in ten cities across the country with a record contract on the line, as well as the possibility of an acting gig with the Japanese film studio Shōchiku Kinema. Hye Eun Choi writes that in the 1930s record companies regularly held contests like these "as a means to recruit new artists as well as raise their revenues." [^33] Indeed, the events promoted the companies' brand names and their association with talent, and sometimes sales of a particular hit song or recital. Sponsoring Columbia Records's contest was the Joseon ilbo (Korea daily), which sought to increase sales through regular updates on the proceedings and presented the contestants to readers with a photo in its first major announcement.[^34] The events were held in the evening, and each week time allowed for only three contestants to sing one designated song and one of their own choosing.[^35] Yujeong Jang notes that, because popular music still lacked the reputation of Western classical music, only noted specialists of the latter genre were asked to serve as judges. On this occasion they were Prof. Mary Young of Ewha College, Prof. Jemyeong Hyeon of Yeonheui College, and pianist and soprano Seongdeok Yun.[^36] At the finals, held on February 17, 1934, emerging stars Jeong Ilgyeong and Go Boksu came in first and second place, respectively (see Figure 1.1).

> 在韩国，从 1933 年 10 月起的大约六个月内，Columbia Records 在全国十个城市举办了一场流行音乐歌唱比赛，获胜者不仅能获得唱片合约，还有机会与日本电影公司松竹映画合作演出。Hye Eun Choi 写道，在 1930 年代，唱片公司经常举办此类比赛，“以此作为招募新艺人并增加收入的手段”。[^33] 事实上，这些活动推广了公司的品牌名称及其与才华的关联，有时还促进了某首热门歌曲或独奏会的销售。赞助 Columbia Records 比赛的是《朝鲜日报》，该报通过定期更新比赛进程来增加销量，并在首次重大公告中附上参赛者的照片向读者介绍。[^34] 比赛在晚上举行，每周只允许三名参赛者演唱一首指定歌曲和一首自选歌曲。[^35] Yujeong Jang 指出，由于流行音乐仍缺乏西方古典音乐的声誉，只有后者的知名专家被邀请担任评委。此次比赛的评委包括梨花女子大学的 Mary Young 教授、延禧大学的 Jemyeong Hyeon 教授，以及钢琴家兼女高音 Seongdeok Yun。[^36] 在 1934 年 2 月 17 日举行的决赛中，新星 Jeong Ilgyeong 和 Go Boksu 分别获得第一名和第二名（见图 1.1）。

The success of the scheme prompted other record companies to follow suit. After similar contests had been held by Okeh in 1935–1936 and Victor in early 1939,[^37] on July 29, 1939, Taepyeong Records announced another nationwide contest, again in collaboration with the Joseon ilbo.[^38] This time, however, judges were chosen from among notable figures from the popular music scene, namely composer Jeon Gihyeon and lyricist Cheon Ato. Finalists had to sing Chae Gyuyeop's hit song "Bukkuk ocheon kiro" (The northern country's 5,000 kilometers, Taihei8600), which the company had released in January that year, and one song of their own choosing.[^39] A few months later, it was Polydor Japan's turn to organize a nationwide "New Singer Contest" (Sinin gasu seonbal daehoe), with support from the Donga ilbo (East Asia daily). The contest also played out over six months, but the repertoire was fully predetermined and comprised songs released by Polydor in the year prior: three particular songs for male contestants and three for female ones. Judging was once more done by well-established insiders of the popular music scene: actor and lyricist Pyeong Wang, Polydor's local head of A&R, and composers Mun Howol and Jeon Gihyeon.[^40] In 1942, Taepyeong Records announced what would be the last national pop music singing contest to be held before the end of colonial rule.[^41]

> 该计划的成功促使其他唱片公司纷纷效仿。在 Okeh 于 1935–1936 年以及 Victor 于 1939 年初举办类似比赛后，[^37] 1939 年 7 月 29 日，太平唱片再次与《朝鲜日报》合作，宣布举办另一场全国性比赛。[^38] 然而，这一次的评委从流行音乐界的知名人士中选出，即作曲家全基贤和作词家千阿托。决赛选手必须演唱蔡奎燁的热门歌曲《北国五千里》（Taihei8600），该歌曲由该公司于当年 1 月发行，以及一首自选歌曲。[^39] 几个月后，轮到日本 Polydor 在《东亚日报》的支持下，组织一场全国性的“新歌手选拔大赛”。该比赛同样持续了六个月，但曲目完全预先确定，包括 Polydor 在之前一年发行的歌曲：男选手三首特定歌曲，女选手三首特定歌曲。评审工作再次由流行音乐界资深内部人士担任：演员兼作词家平王、Polydor 本地艺人及 repertoire 部门负责人，以及作曲家文浩月和全基贤。[^40] 1942 年，太平唱片宣布举办殖民统治结束前的最后一场全国流行音乐歌唱比赛。[^41]

Noted vocalists who were "discovered" through talent shows include Ilgyeong Jeong, Boksu Go, Sejeong Jang, Geumja Jo, Bangnam Jin, Nana Baek, and Yeongchun Kim.[^42] Hye Eun Choi finds that successful singers like Boksu Go would generally enjoy a greater reputation than their female counterparts.[^43] Although the neoliberal nature of the contest, the idea that anyone could be a star, was intriguing, the negative perception of gisaeng persisted, and not because the young women's music training gave them an unfair advantage. Despite the prevalence of gisaeng in everyday life and the high salaries of some former gisaeng-turned–pop stars, the association of gisaeng with popular music long tainted the latter. The negative association may have led to the term yeoryu myeongchang being introduced. Used to refer to acclaimed gisaeng performers of traditional music, the first part of the term (yeoryu), Choi argues, indicates that the women were educated and "socially successful." [^44] But although the gisaeng past of female stars was thus rarely celebrated, despite the rigorous training they had undergone, women's working-class backgrounds could work in their favor as long as they did not call their virtue into question. That the talent of contestant Jang Sejeong was discovered while she was "working as a salesgirl at Hwasin Department Store in Pyongyang," for example, may have boosted her appeal, especially since department store saleswomen were known to be selected on the basis of their looks.[^45]

> 通过选秀节目“发掘”出的知名歌手包括郑日卿、高福寿、金世正、赵锦子、陈芳男、白娜娜和金永春。[^42] 崔惠恩发现，像高福寿这样成功的男歌手通常比女歌手享有更高的声誉。[^43] 尽管选秀的新自由主义本质——即任何人都能成为明星——颇具吸引力，但对妓生的负面看法依然存在，这并非因为年轻女性的音乐训练赋予了她们不公平的优势。尽管妓生在日常生活中很常见，且一些前妓生转型的流行歌手收入颇丰，但妓生与流行音乐的关联长期玷污了后者。这种负面关联可能导致了“女流名唱”一词的引入。该词用于指代备受赞誉的传统音乐妓生表演者，崔惠恩认为，该词的前半部分“女流”表明这些女性受过教育且“在社会上取得了成功”。[^44] 然而，尽管女明星的妓生过往很少被歌颂——尽管她们经历过严格训练——只要不质疑她们的品德，女性工人阶级的背景反而可能对她们有利。例如，参赛者金世正的才华是在她“于平壤和信百货公司担任售货员时”被发现的，这或许提升了她的吸引力，尤其是因为百货公司售货员以相貌选拔而闻名。[^45]

Similar to the popular Korea's Got Talent–like TV shows of today, the contests entailed the public auditioning of contestants performing a limited range of music in popular styles in front of a panel of people working for the record company and a collaborating entertainment agency.[^46] The range of well-established pop songs was much more limited at the time, so in order to promote their own productions and have them regarded as the standard, contestants were required to cover the record companies' own hit songs. From a marketing point of view, little could go wrong, but the commercial importance of the talent shows still cast doubts upon the selection criteria applied. Despite its own involvement in the formula from the start, on April 25, 1940, the Joseon ilbo published an editorial that criticized the commercialism of talent shows, arguing that it was wrong of record companies to ask contestants to buy two records and pay a participation fee.[^47] Since it did not mention which company it had in mind, the critique is likely to have been aimed at Taepyeong Records and Polydor, which had both charged an application fee of 3 won the previous year. Amounting to the cost of a record, it was a relatively high fee that may have been partly intended as a form of preselection due to the high number of applications.[^48] A month later, the paper published another critique of the shows, this time focused on the absence of proper criteria and bias toward pretty female contestants and those with aegyo (winsomeness).[^49] Criticism of the alleged bias was likely fueled by the looks of female contestants frequently coming up in public discourse, as well as by the faces of both male and female entertainers having been customarily printed on flyers, newspaper advertisements, and posters since the mid-1920s. Since the emphasis on the looks of female entertainers was not limited to the shows or the record industry, a critique like this could not have much effect. A newspaper report on Taepyeong Records's second contest in early 1942 lists the songs – all Taihei hits – on which the contestants would be judged, but again omits any discussion of criteria.[^50]

> 与当今流行的韩国达人秀类电视节目类似，这些比赛要求参赛者在唱片公司工作人员及合作娱乐公司组成的评审团面前进行公开试镜，表演有限范围内流行风格的歌曲。[^46] 当时广为流传的流行歌曲范围十分有限，因此为了推广自家作品并使之成为标准，参赛者被要求翻唱唱片公司自己的热门歌曲。从营销角度来看，这几乎不会出错，但选秀节目的商业重要性仍让人对其选拔标准产生质疑。尽管《朝鲜日报》从一开始就参与了这种模式，但在 1940 年 4 月 25 日，该报发表了一篇社论，批评选秀节目的商业主义，认为唱片公司要求参赛者购买两张唱片并支付参与费是不合理的。[^47] 由于未指明具体公司，这篇批评很可能针对的是太平唱片和 Polydor，这两家公司在前一年都收取了 3 韩元的申请费。这笔费用相当于一张唱片的价格，相对较高，部分原因可能是由于申请人数众多，以此作为一种预选方式。[^48] 一个月后，该报又发表了一篇对选秀节目的批评，这次聚焦于缺乏适当标准以及对漂亮女参赛者和有“aegyo”（可爱魅力）者的偏见。[^49] 对所谓偏见的批评很可能源于女性参赛者的外貌频繁出现在公共讨论中，以及自 20 世纪 20 年代中期以来，男女艺人的面孔习惯性地印在传单、报纸广告和海报上。由于对女性艺人外貌的强调并不局限于选秀节目或唱片行业，这样的批评难以产生太大影响。1942 年初，一篇关于太平唱片第二次比赛的报纸报道列出了参赛者将被评判的歌曲——均为 Taihei 的热门歌曲——但再次省略了对评判标准的任何讨论。[^50]

Critics of the talent shows and the social hierarchies they upheld could not overturn their own role as consumers, let alone their status as colonized subjects. They were reminded of this on May 22, 1933, when the government promulgated the Gramophone Record Regulations (Chugeumgi rekodeu chwiche gyuchik), which banned many albums for being disturbing or immoral.[^51] But the shared recognition of raw talent, that subjective social standard that had been cultivated over years, may have felt no less magical than finding the perfect partner.[^52] Although the format invited the opinions of privileged citizens too, the audiences' efforts to serve cultural justice by giving recognition to amateur music talents brought the colonized together over matters of taste.[^53] The shows were premised, after all, on individuals' mastery in their popular culture. Especially when living under colonial rule, finding commonalities in defiance of the political hegemony, the formula's cultivation of shared norms, would have been gratifying. Apart from the element of public scrutiny, the talent shows' native embedding of authenticity was crucial. Annette Hill posits that "The more emphasis is placed on spectacle and style, the more audiences look for authenticity in people's behavior, emotions and the settings for representations of reality."[^54] Although the record industry had not yet been hit by major controversies or scandals, the decades-long seeming absence of the general public's immediate participation led some to question the notion of "popular" (yuhaeng) entertainment regardless of the involvement of Koreans in the creative production process.[^55]

> 对选秀节目及其所维护的社会等级制度持批评态度的人，无法推翻自己作为消费者的角色，更不用说他们作为被殖民者的身份了。1933 年 5 月 22 日，政府颁布了《留声机唱片管理条例》（Chugeumgi rekodeu chwiche gyuchik），禁止了许多被认为具有扰乱性或违背道德的专辑，这让他们意识到了这一点。[^51] 然而，对原始天赋的共同认可——这种经过多年培养的主观社会标准——或许与找到完美伴侣一样神奇。[^52] 尽管这种形式也邀请了特权公民的意见，但观众通过认可业余音乐人才来维护文化正义的努力，将殖民地人民在品味问题上团结在了一起。[^53] 毕竟，这些节目以个人在流行文化中的掌握程度为前提。尤其是在殖民统治下，当人们寻求共同点以反抗政治霸权时，这种模式对共同规范的培养会令人感到满足。除了公众审视的元素外，选秀节目对真实性的本土化嵌入至关重要。Annette Hill 认为：“越强调奇观和风格，观众就越会在人们的行为、情感以及再现现实的场景中寻找真实性。”[^54] 尽管唱片行业尚未受到重大争议或丑闻的冲击，但几十年来普通民众似乎缺乏直接参与，这导致一些人质疑“流行”（yuhaeng）娱乐的概念，无论韩国人是否参与了创意生产过程。[^55]

Despite Koreans having no control over any particular medium or genre and the judging catering to a Korean audience, the talent shows were a platform for uniquely Korean sentiments and highlighted the potential of the subordinated. That finalists would have a chance of success with Japanese and Japanese colonial subjects overseas was important for Koreans' self-image. But although an article in the Maeil sinbo from June 1925 boasted that "Korean [traditional] vocal music will reverberate in foreign ears,"[^56] the reality was that outside Korea, few people were interested in Korean renditions of Western music. Only a handful of Korean stars enjoyed success in Japan, where in 1940, Korean music made up less than 2–3 percent of all records produced.[^57] Records released under a Japanese pseudonym were either Japanese translations of Korean songs or songs specifically written for the Japanese market. While a few Korean virtuosos were able to briefly escape their colonial subjectivity in Japan by working under a pseudonym, Western audiences would not look beyond their ethnicity. Korea's foremost celebrity, dancer Choe Seung-hui, for example, found that foreign audiences did not care much for Asians performing Western dance, in original or adapted forms. In spite of her wide range of styles, they preferred an "oriental" musical accompaniment to one involving Western instruments, which forced her to find Korean Americans to accompany her on Korean traditional instruments during her tour in the United States.[^58]

> 尽管韩国人无法控制任何特定的媒介或类型，且评审标准迎合韩国观众，但这些才艺节目成为了展现独特韩国情感的舞台，并凸显了被支配群体的潜力。决赛选手有机会在日本及海外日本殖民地的观众中获得成功，这对韩国人的自我形象至关重要。然而，尽管 1925 年 6 月《每日新报》上的一篇文章宣称“韩国[传统]声乐将在外国听众中回响”，[^56] 现实却是，在韩国之外，几乎没有人对韩国人演绎的西方音乐感兴趣。只有少数韩国明星在日本取得了成功，而 1940 年，韩国音乐仅占日本所有唱片产量的不到 2% 至 3%。[^57] 以日本化名发行的唱片，要么是韩国歌曲的日文翻译版，要么是专门为日本市场创作的歌曲。尽管少数韩国艺术家能通过使用化名在日本短暂摆脱殖民身份，但西方观众始终无法超越他们的种族视角。例如，韩国最著名的明星、舞蹈家 Choe Seung-hui 发现，外国观众对亚洲人表演西方舞蹈（无论是原创还是改编形式）并不太感兴趣。尽管她风格多样，但观众更偏爱“东方”音乐伴奏而非西方乐器，这迫使她在美国巡演期间寻找韩裔美国人用韩国传统乐器为她伴奏。[^58]

### Conclusion
**结论**

As the above examples demonstrate, fandom in colonial Korea comprised a very complex range of emotions and aspirations that deserves much more careful attention than is paid here. Apart from infatuation with a celebrity or the social distinction that music could provide, from early on pop music fandom was driven by the beauty of the melodies and lyrics and the music's association with a modern, Western view on life. Fandom was liberating, as it appealed to modern sensibilities around class, gender, and community. By lending them a voice and highlighting their economic and cultural capital, it gave fans a sense of empowerment. Although foreign companies seldom acknowledged either the talent of a Korean pop star or the importance of their Korean fans, they allowed fans to imagine a connection to peers outside their social circle and provided opportunities to collect experiences and items that quickly grew in prestige. The careers of some female singers were launched by the neoliberal "stick it to the man" formula that proved popular with many Koreans, but misogyny prevailed. Some Koreans were concerned about the degrading effect of gisaeng's prominence on the sanctity of Korean pop entertainment, but not equally concerned about traditional forms of entertainment. This may be in part because the latter did not invite comparison with Japanese or Western entertainment and in part because of the notion that women trained as courtesans ought not to represent modern Korea. It is ironic, because eroticism helped promote Korean popular culture among both Koreans and Japanese, and it frequently relied on association with the gisaeng institution.

> 如上述例子所示，殖民地韩国的粉丝文化包含了一系列极为复杂的情感和愿望，值得比此处更细致的关注。除了对名人的迷恋或音乐所能提供的社会地位外，流行音乐粉丝文化从一开始就受到旋律与歌词之美，以及音乐与现代西方生活观关联的驱动。粉丝文化具有解放性，因为它诉诸围绕阶级、性别和社群的现代情感。通过赋予粉丝声音并凸显其经济与文化资本，粉丝文化给予他们一种赋权感。尽管外国公司很少承认韩国流行歌星的才华或韩国粉丝的重要性，但它们让粉丝得以想象与社交圈外同龄人的联系，并提供机会收集迅速增值的经历与物品。一些女歌手的职业生涯是通过新自由主义的“反抗权威”模式开启的，这种模式深受许多韩国人欢迎，但厌女情绪依然盛行。部分韩国人担忧妓生的突出地位对韩国流行娱乐的圣洁性产生堕落影响，但对传统娱乐形式却未给予同等关注。这或许部分是因为后者无法与日本或西方娱乐相提并论，部分是因为人们认为受训为艺妓的女性不应代表现代韩国。具有讽刺意味的是，情色元素既帮助韩国流行文化在韩国人和日本人中推广，又常常依赖于与妓生制度的关联。

Although opportunities to become a professional entertainer with one of the largely Japanese-owned businesses were relatively small in number, already in the 1920s aspiring artists managed to cultivate their unique performing skills and to land contracts by way of private auditions, public talent shows, and radio gigs. The small- to medium-sized events groomed talents to serve both Korean and foreign audiences, singing songs in Korean and Japanese, with communication skills and looks undoubtedly playing a significant role. Once they secured a record contract, their lives could change dramatically. When Columbia chose to organize its own show nationwide, it did not announce any judging criteria. It wanted the audience to become involved and intended to use the momentum the shows built to sell records. The judges were mostly industry insiders, or at least part of an elite circle of music experts, and well aware of the commercial risk involved in voting against the unwritten criteria. By collaborating with major newspapers, the companies involved had nigh full control over the selection process and outcome. Audience members would have had their share of criticism but no public outlet for their voice. Since the few critical editorials that made it to print targeted no record company by name, they may have served to promote rather than discredit the shows that were still running.

> 尽管成为日本大型娱乐公司旗下专业艺人的机会相对有限，但早在 1920 年代，有抱负的艺术家们就通过私人试镜、公开才艺秀和广播演出等方式，成功培养了自己独特的表演技巧并获得了合约。中小型活动培养的人才既服务韩国观众也服务外国观众，他们用韩语和日语演唱歌曲，沟通技巧和外貌无疑在其中发挥了重要作用。一旦他们获得唱片合约，生活就可能发生翻天覆地的变化。当 Columbia 选择在全国范围内举办自己的演出时，它并未公布任何评判标准。它希望观众参与其中，并打算利用演出积累的势头来销售唱片。评委大多是业内人士，或者至少是音乐专家精英圈的一部分，他们深知违背不成文标准进行投票所涉及的商业风险。通过与主要报纸合作，相关公司几乎完全掌控了选拔过程和结果。观众可能会有自己的批评意见，但没有公开发声的渠道。由于少数见诸报端的批评性社论并未点名任何唱片公司，它们可能反而起到了宣传作用，而非损害仍在进行的演出的声誉。

After Liberation, music talent shows did not disappear, but those that ran did so without the involvement of any major record company or media outlet. Record sales were small, and the only radio station to frequently play pop music was that of the American Forces Korea Network (AFKN). Rather than using amateur radio shows as a springboard, aspiring artists would audition privately with the entertainment division of the Eighth United States Army in Korea (EUSAK), where good movement and showmanship outweighed musical skill. Successful entertainers could land a gig headlining the prestigious Walker Hill Shows (1962–2002) or appear on TBC's highly popular TV program Show Show Show (1964–1983). That the latter often featured talents from the military circuit was due to their ability to move well on stage and thus on camera.[^59] Rather than good musicianship, therefore, from the 1960s Korean pop music began to prioritize good movement and showmanship, fostered partly by the expressiveness of the Western music genres popular on the military circuit. The cultural and political importance of the military entertainment circuit is exemplified by Korean pop idols the Kim Sisters migrating to the United States in the early 1960s in pursuit of the American Dream. It signaled an embrace of American culture and of a neoliberal, capitalist model of economic development that foregrounds individualism rather than defiance of any hegemonic superstructure. But Koreans would eventually develop a major, global entertainment industry of their own. And it is its hegemony that the industry's own talent shows are now meant to thwart.

> 解放后，音乐选秀节目并未消失，但那些仍在运营的节目都没有任何大型唱片公司或媒体机构的参与。唱片销量很小，唯一经常播放流行音乐的广播电台是驻韩美军广播网（AFKN）。有抱负的艺人不会将业余广播节目作为跳板，而是私下向驻韩美军第八集团军（EUSAK）的娱乐部门试镜，在那里，良好的动作和表演技巧比音乐技能更重要。成功的艺人可以获得在著名的 Walker Hill 秀（1962–2002）中担任主打的演出机会，或者出现在 TBC 极受欢迎的电视节目《Show Show Show》（1964–1983）中。后者之所以经常展示来自军队巡回演出的才艺，是因为他们能够在舞台上，进而在镜头前灵活移动。[^59] 因此，从 1960 年代开始，韩国流行音乐不再优先考虑良好的音乐素养，而是开始重视良好的动作和表演技巧，这部分得益于军队巡回演出中流行的西方音乐类型的表现力。军队娱乐巡回演出的文化和政治重要性，体现在韩国流行偶像金氏姐妹于 1960 年代初移居美国追求美国梦这一事件上。这标志着对美国文化的接纳，以及对一种强调个人主义而非反抗任何霸权上层建筑的新自由主义、资本主义经济发展模式的拥抱。但韩国人最终会发展出自己庞大的全球娱乐产业。而该产业自身的选秀节目现在正是为了挫败其霸权。

Today's talent shows retain many of the elements of control: the lack of clear criteria, the record contract on the line, the momentum built over months of auditions, the involvement of major media outlets, and neoliberalist self-promotion by the artists. Among some of the new elements are the primacy of backstories and the selection of judges based on their ability to give strong, theatrical performances in supporting or critiquing performances.60 While the sociopolitical conditions of today's shows are vastly different from those in the past, the notion of talent remains largely unquestioned. Despite criticism of commercial products and schemes being commonplace, the talent shows are supported by the backstories' evidence of authenticity and continue to compel viewers to participate in the voting process in the interest of preserving merit-based selection. However, the notion of "talent" still adheres merely to a skill set that "fits" a set of long-cultivated standards. While the desire to generate greater control over one's popular culture may be less than it was under colonial rule, deciding with many others on presumed authenticity and quality may allow voters to find unity in a common purpose, promote their fandom and ability for scrutiny, or compete with fans of other idols. It may be a neoliberalist trap, but the format does represent the voices and aesthetics of a majority and provide genuine business opportunities to individual artists. I suspect, therefore, that we may all be stuck to this man for some time yet.

> 如今的选秀节目保留了许多控制元素：缺乏明确标准、悬而未决的唱片合约、数月海选积累的势头、主流媒体的参与，以及艺人奉行的新自由主义自我推广。一些新元素包括背景故事的首要地位，以及评委的选拔标准——依据他们能否在支持或点评表演时，给出强烈、戏剧化的表现。[^60] 尽管当今选秀节目的社会政治条件与过去大相径庭，但“天赋”这一概念在很大程度上仍未被质疑。尽管对商业产品和模式的批评屡见不鲜，但选秀节目凭借背景故事提供的真实性证据，继续迫使观众为了维护择优选拔而参与投票过程。然而，“天赋”这一概念仍然仅仅依附于一套“符合”长期培养标准的技能。虽然人们对自己流行文化产生更大控制权的欲望可能不如殖民统治时期强烈，但与他人共同决定所谓的真实性和质量，或许能让投票者在共同目标中找到团结，推广他们的粉丝文化和审视能力，或与其他偶像的粉丝竞争。这可能是一个新自由主义陷阱，但这种形式确实代表了多数人的声音和审美，并为个体艺人提供了真正的商业机会。因此，我怀疑，我们可能还要与这个人纠缠一段时间。
